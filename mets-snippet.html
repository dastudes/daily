There are mornings when a one-run win on the West Coast feels like more than a one-run win, and this is one of them. The Mets are not a good baseball team right now — eleven and twenty-one tells you that, and so does the eleven-and-a-half-game gulf between them and the Braves — but in Anaheim on Friday night they did the thing that struggling teams must occasionally do to keep the season from curdling: they came from behind, late, against a team they were supposed to beat, and they handed the ball to their best relievers and watched them close it out. The total WPA swing of 2.58 marks it as a genuinely contested evening, the kind of game the Mets have lost more often than won this year.

The shape of the night belonged to Christian Scott [5 IP, 2 ER, 8 K | 4.43 ERA, PAR 1], who was excellent through five and gave the lineup a chance to do something it has rarely done — which is rally. The sixth inning is where the game turned, with Juan Soto [1-3, 1 BB | RC 15] and Francisco Alvarez [1-4, 1 RBI | RC 11] crossing on a Marcus Semien [1-4, 2 RBI | RC 9] single, and an inning later Ronny Mauricio [1-3, 1 HR | RC 2] added the insurance run that wound up being the difference. Bo Bichette [2-4 | RC 12] had the kind of multi-hit night that has been quietly consistent for him in this otherwise punchless lineup. From there it was a parade — Huascar Brazobán [1 IP, 0 ER, 1 K | 1.28 ERA, PAR 6], Brooks Raley [1 IP, 0 ER, 1 K | 1.61 ERA, PAR 5], Luke Weaver [1 IP, 0 ER, 2 K | 5.54 ERA, PAR 1], and Devin Williams [1 IP, 0 ER, 2 K | 7.20 ERA, PAR 1], each handed an inning, each handing back a zero. It is worth noting how rarely this team has been able to script a game so cleanly.

The standings, of course, do not soften. The Mets remain in the basement of the NL East, and the news from elsewhere in the division was almost uniformly bad: Atlanta won again, extending its lead atop the division; Philadelphia kept its quiet four-game streak alive; even the Marlins and Nationals, who lost, are ahead of New York in the table. Only the Braves are running away with anything — they sit at twenty-three and ten, with a run differential that dwarfs the rest of the division — but the more pressing fact for Mets fans is that four teams stand between them and first place, not one.

The Pythagorean record offers neither comfort nor alarm. At 11.8 expected wins against 11 actual, the Mets are right about where their run differential says they should be — which is to say, this is not a team being robbed by sequencing or bullpen collapses. They are hitting like the worst offense in the division (an OPS of .631 tells that story plainly), and the pitching, encouragingly, has been better than the results suggest — a team FIP of 3.63 is real, and Friday's effort from Scott and the bullpen is consistent with that. If there is a path back to respectability, it runs through the arms. The bats will have to find themselves.

Among individuals worth holding onto: Devin Williams has settled into the closer's role with the kind of quiet authority the Mets paid for, and the late-inning trio of Brazobán, Raley, and Weaver is shaping up as something genuinely useful. Soto continues to grind out competitive at-bats even in a lineup that does not always reward him for it; his walk on Friday night was the sort of small, professional thing that gets lost in box scores but matters over a hundred and sixty-two games. There is a long way back from eleven-and-a-half out on the first of May. But they won a game last night, and tonight they get to try again.
