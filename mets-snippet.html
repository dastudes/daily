Mets Morning Briefing — Thursday, May 14, 2026

There is a particular kind of Citi Field night that asks more of its patrons than baseball usually does — the slow kind, the kind where the scoreboard stays stingy and every half-inning seems to deepen the silence rather than break it. Last night was one of those. The Mets and Tigers traded a run apiece in the early frames and then settled into a long, breath-held argument that wouldn't resolve itself until the tenth. Christian Scott [4.2 IP, 2 ER, 5 K | 3.55 ERA, PAR 4] gave the Mets what they needed and not quite what they wanted, working into the fifth before handing things over to a bullpen that simply refused to break. Huascar Brazobán [2.1 IP, 0 ER, 2 K | 2.14 ERA, PAR 7], Luke Weaver [1 IP, 0 ER, 1 K | 4.21 ERA, PAR 4], Devin Williams [1 IP, 0 ER, 1 K | 5.45 ERA, PAR 3], and finally Brooks Raley [1 IP, 0 ER, 1 K | 1.06 ERA, PAR 8] combined for more than five innings without surrendering a run — the kind of relay performance that, on the right night, becomes the whole story. Carson Benge [3-5 | RC 13]'s single in the bottom of the tenth ended things, with A.J. Ewing [0-3, 1 BB | RC 2] crossing the plate. Earlier, in the seventh, Bo Bichette [1-4 | RC 13] had driven in Luis Torrens [1-3, 1 BB | RC 4] to tie it. That was the game: two small, necessary acts of contact in a contest that demanded patience.

It was, by the WPA arithmetic, the kind of game that turns on a single swing, and you could feel that all night. Benge had himself an evening at the plate, the sort of three-hit afternoon that doesn't always show up in highlights but does show up in wins. Framber Valdez [6.2 IP, 2 ER, 7 K | 4.32 ERA, PAR 10] was excellent for Detroit, which made the Mets' inability to break through for so long less an indictment than a fact of the matter. When Kenley Jansen [1 IP, 0 ER, 3 K | 4.46 ERA, PAR 1] came on in the ninth and struck out the side, the game felt as though it might tilt the other way. It didn't. The bullpen held; the lineup, eventually, found a sliver.

And yet the standings, those flat and unforgiving columns, remind us where the Mets actually live. They are 17-25, twelve and a half games behind Atlanta in the NL East, last in a division the Braves have begun to make their private property. The good news, such as it is, comes in modest increments: two in a row now, six of the last ten, a Pythagorean record that suggests they've been only marginally unlucky rather than systematically betrayed. Their run differential is unflattering but not catastrophic. Their team FIP is, in fact, the best in the division — better than Atlanta's. The arms are working. The bats are not.

The rest of the division did them no real favors and no real harm. Atlanta beat the Cubs again and continues its march, now thirty wins to thirteen losses, a pace that has stopped looking like a hot start and begun looking like an identity. Washington's wild win in Cincinnati — a five-WPA-swing affair that turned on Daylen Lile [2-4, 1 HR | RC 25]'s tenth-inning homer — keeps the Nationals nominally relevant at .488. Philadelphia lost at Fenway, despite a strong outing from Andrew Painter [5 IP, 1 ER, 4 K | 6.29 ERA, PAR 2], and Miami's offensive eruption at Target Field pulled the Marlins even with the Phillies. The middle of this division is a knot of mediocre teams; the top is unreachable from where the Mets sit, at least for now.

What's worth holding onto, on a morning like this: the pitching is real. Nolan McLean sits third in the NL in FIP. The bullpen, last night, was as good as bullpens get. And the lineup, while quiet, has its quiet contributors — Benge chief among them last night, Torrens working a walk and scoring, Mark Vientos [1-3, 1 BB | RC 14] drawing his free pass and crossing the plate. A win is a win. Two in a row is the start of something or it is nothing at all, and we won't know which for a few weeks yet. The Mets are not where anyone wanted them to be in mid-May. But they played a game last night that good teams play, and they won it. That isn't everything. It also isn't nothing.
