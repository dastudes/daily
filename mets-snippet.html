Good morning. There is no kind way to put this: the Mets lost a game in Anaheim that was, by every available measure, theirs to take, and they lost it in the cruelest fashion the schedule allows — extra innings, on the road, on a walkoff single by a player most Mets fans couldn't have picked out of a lineup card a week ago. The 4.48 WPA swing tells you everything about the shape of the night: a game that lurched between hope and dread, that the Mets led, that they held, that slipped away in the seventh, and that finally ended in the tenth when Oswald Peraza [3-5 | RC 16] of the Angels brought the ghost runner home. Reid Detmers [6.1 IP, 3 ER, 8 K | 4.28 ERA, PAR 11] gave the Angels length, Nolan McLean [4 IP, 3 ER, 6 K | 2.99 ERA, PAR 15] gave the Mets a competitive start before handing things off, and the bullpen — Tobias Myers [2.2 IP, 0 ER, 0 K | 2.05 ERA, PAR 8], Huascar Brazobán [1.1 IP, 0 ER, 1 K | 1.18 ERA, PAR 7], even Craig Kimbrel [1 IP, 0 ER, 1 K | 4.43 ERA, PAR 2] — kept the line clean until Austin Warren [0.2 IP, 0 ER, 0 K | 2.20 ERA, PAR 1] could not. There is no shame in losing this kind of game. There is also no comfort in it.

Within the broader gloom, a few Mets quietly did their jobs. Austin Slater [2-3 | RC 3] had the cleanest night at the plate among the regulars, and Tyrone Taylor [1-4 | RC 5], Mark Vientos [1-4 | RC 8], and Marcus Semien [1-4 | RC 9] each contributed the small pieces of what should have been a winning offensive evening. Juan Soto [1-4, 1 BB | RC 15] drew his walk, as Juan Soto does, and McLean — whose season-long numbers continue to place him among the more interesting young arms in the National League — struck out the side's worth of Angels through four before tiring. None of it added up. That is the story of this team in May: parts that look serviceable in isolation, a whole that keeps coming up a run short.

The standings are what they are, and they are not improving. At 11-22, the Mets sit twelve and a half games behind Atlanta in the NL East, having lost six of their last ten and now riding a fresh losing streak after the Anaheim defeat. The Braves, who beat Colorado handily behind Chris Sale [7 IP, 1 ER, 11 K | 2.14 ERA, PAR 15] and another loud night from Drake Baldwin [3-4, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 28], are pulling away from the division at a pace that ought to concern even the most patient observer. Miami won again, behind seven shutout innings from Max Meyer [7 IP, 0 ER, 7 K | 2.68 ERA, PAR 13], and Washington lost to Milwaukee. The only NL East team beneath the Mets in the standings is no one. They are alone at the bottom, and the gap to fourth-place Philadelphia is widening rather than closing.

The Pythagorean record offers no rescue, either. The Mets' run differential suggests they should be roughly a game better than they are — a rounding error, not a hidden contender waiting to emerge. Their team OPS is the worst in the division by a meaningful margin, and while their pitching peripherals (a team FIP of 3.60) hint that the staff is doing real work, the offense isn't holding up its end. You can see it in last night's box score: ten singles spread across nine hitters, no extra-base hits to speak of, three runs that felt like they took a great deal of effort to produce. A lineup with Soto and Bo Bichette [1-5 | RC 12] and Francisco Alvarez [1-5 | RC 11] and Vientos should not be this quiet this often.

What's left, for now, is the long view and the small consolations. McLean continues to look like a building block. Soto is Soto. The season is young enough that mathematical dignity remains intact, even if the standings have begun to read like a verdict. But twelve and a half games is twelve and a half games, and the Braves show no inclination to slow down. The work in front of this team is no longer about catching anyone. It is about giving its better players reasons to keep playing hard, and giving the rest of us reasons to keep watching. Last night offered neither. Tonight is another chance.
