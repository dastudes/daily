Mets Morning Briefing — Saturday, May 9, 2026

Out in the desert, on a night that asked for patience and got it, the Mets finally won a game by refusing to lose one. Nine innings produced a single run for each side — Mark Vientos [2-4, 1 HR | RC 13] answering Nolan Arenado [1-4, 1 HR | RC 17]'s second-inning home run with one of his own — and then the bullpens took over, and the zeroes piled up like chairs stacked at closing time. Nolan McLean [6 IP, 1 ER, 6 K | 2.79 ERA, PAR 17] was excellent through six, and the relief corps that followed him — Luke Weaver [1 IP, 0 ER, 1 K | 4.47 ERA, PAR 3], Brooks Raley [1 IP, 0 ER, 0 K | 1.20 ERA, PAR 7], Devin Williams [1 IP, 0 ER, 1 K | 5.90 ERA, PAR 3], and Tobias Myers [1 IP, 0 ER, 2 K | 3.49 ERA, PAR 5] in the tenth — surrendered nothing. When the breakthrough came in extras, it came from Vientos again, with a double, and Carson Benge [1-3, 1 BB | RC 10] behind him with another. A 2.99 WPA swing tells you the game was tight all the way through; the box score tells you it was tighter than that.

It is a win, and wins are not to be sneered at when you are 15-23 and looking up at four other teams in your division. The Mets remain in last place in the NL East, ten and a half games behind Atlanta, but their last ten now reads 6-4, and their Pythagorean record suggests they have been roughly the team their run differential says they are — neither cursed nor blessed, just a little short. The pitching has actually been quite good; the team FIP is the best in the division. The OPS is the worst in the division by a comfortable margin. That is the whole story, more or less, and last night was a miniature of it: nine innings of fine pitching, one run of offense, and a rescue mission in the tenth.

The division did the Mets a small favor and a larger one. Atlanta lost in Los Angeles — Chris Sale [7 IP, 2 ER, 7 K | 2.20 ERA, PAR 17] pitched well enough to win most nights and didn't — which means the Braves' lead over the field stayed roughly where it was. Washington beat Miami behind a superb outing from Foster Griffin [7 IP, 1 ER, 9 K | 2.14 ERA, PAR 15], and the Nationals are now the clear second-place team, riding a three-game streak. Philadelphia, meanwhile, suffered the indignity of an extra-inning loss to Colorado after Justin Crawford [3-4, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 16]'s eighth-inning home run had seemingly turned the game. The Phillies and Marlins are tied with the Mets' nearest neighbors at 17-22. None of this changes much for New York in the immediate sense — ten and a half is ten and a half — but it does suggest a division in which only Atlanta is playing like a contender, and that is a useful thing to know in May.

A word for Vientos, who carried the offense almost single-handedly, and for McLean, whose season continues to quietly demand attention; he is among the National League leaders in both strikeouts and FIP, which is the sort of pairing that tends to mean something real. The Mets do not have many players hitting the way you'd want them to right now — the lineup last night was a parade of hitless four-at-bat lines — but they have arms, and arms keep you in games until the hitting decides to show up. It hasn't yet. There is still time.
