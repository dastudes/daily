Morning Briefing: The Mets

There are few small pleasures in a long season quite like beating the team across town, and fewer still when that team has spent the spring looking down its nose at you from the standings. The Mets took the Subway Series opener at Citi Field, and while a single win in mid-May settles nothing — not the rivalry, not the season, not the larger question of what this team actually is — it was the kind of evening that tells a fan it's still worth tuning in. David Peterson [4 IP, 2 ER, 8 K | 5.43 ERA, PAR 9] worked into and out of trouble through four innings, leaving the heavy lifting to a bullpen that answered the call, and Devin Williams [1 IP, 0 ER, 1 K | 5.07 ERA, PAR 4] closed the door. Carson Benge [3-4 | RC 15] set the table all night from the top of the order, and Juan Soto [2-2, 2 BB | RC 21] did Juan Soto things in the middle of it. The decisive blow came from Mark Vientos [1-4 | RC 15] in the fifth, a two-run double that turned a tied game into one the Mets would not relinquish. Brett Baty [1-4 | RC 14] chipped in earlier with a run-scoring double of his own. It was, by the math of the day, a moderately dramatic affair — not a knife fight, but a game where the leverage moved.

The trouble, as ever, is the view from the standings. Even with the win the Mets sit eleven and a half back of Atlanta in the NL East, fifth in a division where the Braves have built the kind of lead that begins to feel structural rather than circumstantial. Washington and Philadelphia are both eight back at .500, and Miami sits ahead of New York as well. The Mets have played decent baseball over their last ten — they are not, at the moment, in freefall — but they have spent most of the spring carrying a run differential that suggests they are roughly the team their record says they are. The Pythagorean projection has them about a game and a half better than they've performed, which is the statistical way of saying: this is who they've been, more or less, with no hidden luck to repay them.

The division did the Mets no favors on Saturday. Atlanta lost — a small mercy — but to look around the room is to find Washington pounding Baltimore, Philadelphia getting a complete-game shutout from Cristopher Sánchez [9 IP, 0 ER, 13 K | 1.83 ERA, PAR 29] that was as dominant a pitching performance as the National League has produced this month, and Miami surviving a wild extra-inning affair against Tampa Bay. The Phillies are now winners of three straight, the Nationals two, and both have crept to .500 while the Mets remain stuck below it. Catching the Braves at this point would require a run of historic proportions; the more honest project is staying close enough to the wild card conversation that summer means something. That race, too, is crowded.

What's worth singling out from the Mets' side is the work of the rotation and bullpen in aggregate — Peterson, Huascar Brazobán [1.1 IP, 0 ER, 2 K | 2.04 ERA, PAR 8], Brooks Raley [0.2 IP, 0 ER, 1 K | 1.05 ERA, PAR 8], Luke Weaver [2 IP, 0 ER, 2 K | 3.77 ERA, PAR 5], and Williams combined to keep the Yankees to three runs across nine innings, and the team's FIP for the season remains among the better marks in the National League. The pitching has been, quietly, the reason this team has any pulse at all. The bats have been the problem; a team OPS in the .640s is not going to win many divisions, and it is the gap between what the arms have done and what the lineup has produced that explains most of the daylight between the Mets and the teams ahead of them. A night like Saturday — Benge on base, Soto working counts, Vientos and Baty driving balls into the gaps — is what the offense is supposed to look like more often than it has.

It was one game. It was a good one. The standings have not moved in any meaningful way, and the Braves remain the Braves. But there is still a season to play, and the Mets, for an evening at least, looked like a team that remembered it.
