Good morning. There's no soft way to say it: the Mets lost a game in Anaheim on Saturday night that they very much could have won, and the manner of it — ten innings, a walkoff, a 4.48 WPA swing that ranks as the most dramatic finish in baseball yesterday — is the kind of loss that lingers into Sunday's coffee. Nolan McLean [4 IP, 3 ER, 6 K | 2.99 ERA, PAR 15] gave the club a respectable if abbreviated start, and the bullpen behind him — Tobias Myers [2.2 IP, 0 ER, 0 K | 2.05 ERA, PAR 8], Huascar Brazobán [1.1 IP, 0 ER, 1 K | 1.18 ERA, PAR 7], Craig Kimbrel [1 IP, 0 ER, 1 K | 4.43 ERA, PAR 2] — kept the Angels quiet across the middle innings, long enough for the offense to claw back into a tie. But Austin Warren [0.2 IP, 0 ER, 0 K | 2.20 ERA, PAR 1] drew the short straw in the tenth, and Oswald Peraza [3-5 | RC 16] ended the evening with a single up the middle. The Mets had their chances; Reid Detmers [6.1 IP, 3 ER, 8 K | 4.28 ERA, PAR 11] struck out the side's worth of them across his outing, and a tenth-inning double play snuffed out the last real opportunity to steal it.

Around the lineup, Austin Slater [2-3 | RC 3] was the bright spot off the bench, and Tyrone Taylor [1-4 | RC 5]'s seventh-inning RBI single was the kind of small, useful hit this team needs more of. Juan Soto [1-4, 1 BB | RC 15] drew his walk and added a hit; Bo Bichette [1-5 | RC 12], Francisco Alvarez [1-5 | RC 11], Mark Vientos [1-4 | RC 8], and Marcus Semien [1-4 | RC 9] each managed a single piece of contact in long nights at the plate. It wasn't a quiet offensive performance so much as a scattered one — base hits parceled out across nine players, none of whom could string two together at the right moment.

The standings, meanwhile, do not flatter. The Mets sit last in the NL East at 11-22, twelve and a half games behind Atlanta, and their recent ten-game stretch (4-6) suggests neither collapse nor recovery, just the slow grind of a team that hasn't yet found itself. The Pythagorean record pegs them at roughly 12 wins — they're underperforming, but only modestly. The deeper concern is the run differential itself: a team OPS under .630 tells you the bats have not arrived. The pitching, on aggregate, has been fine. The hitting has been the problem, and on Saturday it was the problem again.

The division offered no mercy. Chris Sale [7 IP, 1 ER, 11 K | 2.14 ERA, PAR 15] and the Braves rolled through Colorado behind a vintage strikeout performance and a big day from Drake Baldwin [3-4, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 28], pushing Atlanta to 24-10 and stretching the lead. Miami's Max Meyer [7 IP, 0 ER, 7 K | 2.68 ERA, PAR 13] spun seven shutout innings against the Phillies, and the Marlins now sit a comfortable second. Even Washington, beaten by Milwaukee, remains ahead of New York. Every rival result on Saturday was, in its small way, another inch of distance.

One name worth holding onto: Nolan McLean, whose season-long work has put him among the NL leaders in strikeouts and FIP. He didn't get the win Saturday, and his line was uneven, but he remains the most encouraging young arm on the staff and a reason to keep watching even when the standings ask you not to. Twelve and a half games is a lot of ground in early [4 IP, 5 ER, 3 K | 3.84 ERA, PAR 6] May. It is not, yet, an obituary. But the Mets will need to start winning the close ones — the McLean starts, the extra-inning coin flips — before the rest of the division decides this race without them.
