Mets Morning Briefing — Friday, May 15, 2026

There are nights at Citi Field when the home team plays as if they've just remembered who they were supposed to be all along, and Thursday was one of them. After spotting Detroit an early three-run lead, the Mets answered with a steady, unhurried accumulation — a run here, two runs there, the kind of inning-by-inning gathering of force that suggests competence rather than panic. Nolan McLean [7 IP, 3 ER, 7 K | 2.94 ERA, PAR 18] worked into the seventh and absorbed those early runs without letting them metastasize, which is the unglamorous half of what good starting pitching looks like. By the time Brett Baty [1-3, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 12], Mark Vientos [1-4, 1 HR | RC 15], Marcus Semien [2-3, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 14], Juan Soto [2-5, 1 HR | RC 18], and rookie A.J. Ewing [2-4, 1 HR | RC 4] had each cleared the fence — five home runs in all — the deficit had become a memory and the evening had the easy feel of a game already decided.

That makes three wins in a row, and seven in the last ten, which is the first stretch this season that begins to look like a pattern rather than a few good days strung together. The trouble is the calendar and the standings. At 18-25, the Mets sit eleven and a half games behind Atlanta in the NL East, which is the kind of deficit that does not yield to streaks so much as to seasons. The Pythagorean record suggests they've been a touch unlucky — the underlying numbers point to a team perhaps a game or two better than its ledger — but unlucky and bad share a zip code this far under .500.

The division did the Mets no favors yesterday, or rather it did them exactly the kind of favor that doesn't matter. Atlanta lost to the Cubs in a tidy shutout, Chris Sale [6 IP, 0 ER, 8 K | 1.96 ERA, PAR 21] pitching beautifully and getting nothing for it, while Washington was dismantled in Cincinnati and Miami was handled in Minneapolis. Only Philadelphia, whose late rally at Fenway felt like the most consequential swing of the night, gained ground. So the Mets picked up a game on four of the five teams ahead of them — and still lost ground, in a sense, because Atlanta keeps doing Atlanta things even on the days they lose, and eleven and a half is eleven and a half. The wild card, with Philadelphia and Washington both hovering around .500 and Miami just ahead, remains the more honest conversation.

What's worth holding onto from Thursday isn't the margin but the distribution. Five different Mets homered, which speaks to a lineup that, on its better nights, doesn't need any one hitter to carry it. Soto continues his quiet, professional work in the middle of the order. Semien, brought in to stabilize the infield, looked the part. And Ewing, getting one of his first real looks, joined the parade — the kind of small moment a rebuilding stretch occasionally offers as compensation. McLean's outing, meanwhile, was the sort of start a team in the Mets' position needs to bank: long, efficient enough, and decisive about not letting a bad first inning become a bad night.

None of this changes the larger arithmetic. But baseball seasons are long enough that even a team eleven and a half games out gets to feel, for a few days, like it has found something. The Mets have won three straight and scored nine runs at home on a Thursday in May. That is not a pennant race. It is, however, a reason to keep watching, which is more than the standings alone would suggest.
