Mets Morning Briefing — Wednesday, May 13, 2026

There are nights at Citi Field that feel less like baseball and more like accounting — a ledger of small credits patiently accumulated until the sum becomes unanswerable. Tuesday was one of those. The Mets did their work in fragments, a run here, a pair there, then three more, then two, then two again, the kind of distributed scoring that suggests a lineup taking turns rather than waiting for someone to play hero. Freddy Peralta [6 IP, 2 ER, 7 K | 3.12 ERA, PAR 14] gave them the kind of start a struggling team craves — long enough to bury the bullpen's anxieties, sharp enough that Dillon Dingler [1-3, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 18]'s early homer for Detroit never grew into a threat. By the time Brooks Raley [1 IP, 0 ER, 1 K | 1.13 ERA, PAR 7] and Austin Warren [2 IP, 0 ER, 3 K | 0.82 ERA, PAR 5] arrived, the game's WPA swing had already settled into the low foothills of decided things.

The offense, so often the source of the season's discontent, was democratic to the point of generosity. Carson Benge [2-5 | RC 11], Mark Vientos [2-5 | RC 13], and Brett Baty [2-5 | RC 10] all chipped in multi-hit nights, Juan Soto [2-5 | RC 16] added two more, and A.J. Ewing [1-2, 3 BB | RC 2] worked three walks atop a hit — the sort of patient afternoon that doesn't show up in a highlight reel but bends a scoreboard all the same. Bo Bichette [1-4, 1 BB | RC 13] reached on the kind of fielder's choice that becomes a turning point when the other team helps you with a throwing error, and the inning got away from Detroit in a hurry. Ten runs is ten runs, even against a Tigers staff that emptied its bullpen in service of damage control.

And yet — and there is always an "and yet" with this team — the Mets remain twelve and a half back in the NL East, last in a five-team race that long ago became a one-team race. The Braves keep winning (three straight now), the Phillies keep winning (three straight of their own), the Nationals took their own game, and even the Marlins, despite a loss, sit ahead of New York in the standings. A 10-2 win is balm; it is not medicine. The division has, for now, moved on without them.

The underlying numbers tell a more interesting story than the standings do. The Mets' Pythagorean record suggests they should be closer to 17-24 — not contention, but not the cellar either — and their team FIP is the best in the division. The pitching, in other words, has been doing its job; it is the offense, with its modest team OPS, that has lagged behind the talent on paper. A night like Tuesday, with hits distributed across the order and Soto looking like Soto again, is the kind of evidence a believer files away. Whether it accumulates into anything is the question that will define the next six weeks.

For now, take the win. Peralta was excellent, the lineup looked whole, and a 10-2 game on a Tuesday in May has a way of feeling, briefly, like the floor of something rather than the ceiling. The Braves are nine games clear of second place and the Mets are three and a half behind that. The math is unkind. But the team is 6-4 over its last ten, and somewhere in there is the outline of a club that might yet be worth watching for reasons beyond habit and affection.
