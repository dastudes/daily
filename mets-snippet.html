The Mets entered the late innings trailing by two, and for a while it seemed they might yet escape the cold clutches of April with something to show for it. MJ Melendez [2-2, 1 HR | RC 8] had staked them to a three-run lead in the third with a three-run shot, and Freddy Peralta [6 IP, 1 ER, 6 K | 3.54 ERA, PAR 10] was working through Washington's lineup with the kind of command that had made him one of the league's most effective starters. But baseball has a way of turning on you in the eighth inning, and when CJ Abrams [2-3, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 24] lifted a two-run homer to right-center off Luke Weaver [1 IP, 2 ER, 1 K | 6.00 ERA, PAR 1], the night went quiet at Citi Field. The Mets threatened in the bottom of the frame—Juan Soto [2-3, 1 BB | RC 15] doubled with one out—but could not push the tying run across. By the ninth, the season's narrative had reasserted itself: another narrow loss, another day closer to the bottom.

The defeat dropped the Mets to 10-21, now eleven and a half games behind Atlanta in the NL East and sinking deeper into last place. They have lost two straight and seven of their last ten, a stretch that has confirmed what the early weeks only hinted at. They sit well below their Pythagorean expectation, which suggests they ought to be 11.2-19.8 instead, though the distinction offers little comfort. The offense remains the persistent problem—a .631 OPS ranks last in the division—and while the pitching has been respectable, it has not been dominant enough to mask the run-scoring drought. Peralta did his part, holding Washington to one earned run over six innings, but that performance now feels like an artifact from a different season.

Philadelphia swept a dramatic doubleheader from San Francisco, winning both games in walk-off fashion and climbing to within two games of the Mets despite their own struggles. Kyle Schwarber [1-2, 2 BB, 1 HR | RC 25] went 4-for-4 in the nightcap, his second home run of the day lifting him to eleven for the season and the top of the National League leaderboard. The Phillies are 12-19 and playing better than their record—overperforming their Pythagorean win total by more than a game—which makes them dangerous in the way that desperate, talented teams often are. Atlanta lost at home to Detroit, ending a stretch of success but hardly denting their commanding lead. Washington, meanwhile, has now won two straight and sits just five games ahead of the Mets, close enough to be a target if one were inclined toward optimism.

What stands out about the Mets is not just that they are losing, but how narrow the margins have become. This was a game with a high WPA swing—a back-and-forth affair that felt winnable until it wasn't. Soto reached base three times, Melendez provided the power, and Peralta gave them length. These are the ingredients of a win, yet the final score reads like so many others this month. The bullpen faltered when it mattered, the offense went silent when it needed to speak, and the Mets are left to wonder how many more of these near-misses they can endure before the season's story's written for good. May begins tomorrow, and with it the hope that something, anything, might shift. But hope is not a strategy, and the standings do not care about almosts.
