Mets Morning Briefing — Sunday, May 10, 2026

There are losses that gut you and losses that simply close, quietly, like a door in a hallway you'd already walked past. Last night in the desert was the latter. The Mets managed three hits, drew their walks, and watched Merrill Kelly [7 IP, 1 ER, 6 K | 7.62 ERA, PAR -3] of the Diamondbacks work seven crisp innings on the other side. Clay Holmes [5.2 IP, 2 ER, 6 K | 1.87 ERA, PAR 18] was, by any honest measure, good — six strikeouts, a single bad sequence in the third when Ildemaro Vargas [1-4 | RC 27] found a hole — but good is not always enough when the offense produces only Brett Baty [1-3 | RC 9]'s double and a smattering of singles from Marcus Semien [1-3 | RC 13] and Tyrone Taylor [1-3 | RC 5]. Juan Soto [0-3, 1 BB | RC 15], Bo Bichette [0-3, 1 BB | RC 13], and MJ Melendez [0-3, 1 BB | RC 9] each drew a walk and otherwise went quietly. One run was all the Mets could conjure, and one run, against a starter in command, is generally the run before the last one.

This is the shape of the Mets' May, and at some point shape becomes identity. They are 15-24, last in the NL East, eleven and a half games behind Atlanta, who keep winning whether anyone is watching or not — last night it was Spencer Strider [6 IP, 0 ER, 8 K | 2.97 ERA, PAR 3] carving up the Dodgers in Los Angeles, the Braves piling on early and never really needing to look back. The Phillies routed Colorado behind a pair of homers from Alec Bohm [3-4, 2 HR | RC 9]. The Marlins outlasted Washington in a genuinely chaotic affair at loanDepot park, the kind of game where the lead changed hands like a rumor. Even the Nationals, who lost, are three and a half games clear of New York. The division has effectively sorted itself, and the Mets are sorting last.

What stings is that the underlying picture isn't quite as bleak as the standings. The Mets' run differential — minus-27 — and their team FIP suggest a club playing roughly a game worse than its talent. The pitching, in aggregate, has been legitimate; Clay Holmes has acquitted himself well in the rotation, and Senga's reliever Jhoan Holmes… well, the league as a whole now sees Holmes of the Mets atop the NL ERA leaderboard, which is its own small consolation. The trouble is the lineup. A team OPS of .633 is not a slump; it is a condition. Until Soto and Bichette and Vientos [0-4 | RC 12] start producing in concert rather than in shifts, the Mets will keep losing the close ones — and last night, in Phoenix, they lost another close one.

The schedule offers no reprieve and no apology. The Diamondbacks are the team they were supposed to handle on a西 swing; instead Arizona has gained a game in the NL West race at New York's expense, which is the sort of detail that tells you where a season stands. Eleven and a half back on May 10 is not mathematically fatal — nothing in May ever is — but it requires the Mets to become something they have not yet been this year: a team whose offense matches its arms. There is time for that. There is, for now, still time. But the calendar, like Paul Sewald [1 IP, 0 ER, 2 K | 3.41 ERA, PAR 3] in the ninth, does not wait around.
