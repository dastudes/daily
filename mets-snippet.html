Mets Morning Briefing — Monday, May 18, 2026

There are wins that arrive cleanly, by daylight, and there are wins like Sunday's — wins that have to be pried loose, inning by inning, from a stubborn rival. The Mets needed ten innings and every available arm and bat to outlast the Yankees at Citi Field, and when the dust cleared they had the second-most volatile game in baseball on the day. Tyrone Taylor [1-3, 1 HR | RC 5]'s three-run shot in the ninth was the swing that bent the game back toward the Mets after a sixth inning in which the Yankees had seized control, and the tenth was won on a quiet bit of business by Marcus Semien [2-5 | RC 16] and Carson Benge [1-5, 1 BB | RC 15]. There is something restorative about beating the Yankees in a Subway Series game in extras. Whatever else this season becomes, that memory belongs to the home crowd now.

The pitching told a more complicated story. Freddy Peralta [5 IP, 3 ER, 4 K | 3.33 ERA, PAR 14] got through five but worked uphill the whole way, scattering walks like loose change, and Sean Manaea [4 IP, 2 ER, 3 K | 6.31 ERA, PAR 2] wasn't crisp either in his four innings of relief work. That the Mets emerged victorious anyway is a credit to Devin Williams [1 IP, 0 ER, 1 K | 4.74 ERA, PAR 4], who held the line in the tenth and earned the win, and to a lineup that kept tacking on at-bats — Marcus Semien, Mark Vientos [2-5 | RC 16], and A.J. Ewing [1-1, 3 BB | RC 5] all reached base multiple times, with Ewing in particular working the count through the afternoon. Juan Soto [1-5 | RC 21]'s ledger was modest in raw terms but he was on base when Taylor's bat did its work in the ninth, which is its own kind of contribution.

And yet. The standings remain unforgiving. Two straight wins lift the spirit but not the position — the Mets are still fourth in the NL East, eleven and a half games behind a Braves team that simply will not cool off. Atlanta sits a comfortable eight games clear of second-place Philadelphia, who themselves are riding a four-game tear. Even Washington sits ahead of the Mets. The math says New York's Pythagorean record is essentially what their actual record is — they are not unlucky so much as simply not quite good enough yet, outscored on the season and middling in the underlying numbers, though the team FIP suggests the pitching has been better than the runs allowed indicate.

That FIP figure is worth holding onto. It is the kind of detail that separates a team treading water from a team quietly drowning, and the Mets, for all their offensive struggles — that team OPS is a sobering thing to look at — are not drowning. The recent stretch (six wins in the last ten) hints at a club finding small footholds. Whether those footholds become a climb or merely a brief pause before another slide is the question that will define the next month. The Braves are not coming back to the field. The wild card, seven games back, is a long-odds proposition that requires several teams ahead of them to falter while you yourself become a different team.

For now, take the Subway Series win for what it is — a Sunday afternoon when a struggling team beat a better one, when Tyrone Taylor turned on a pitch in the ninth inning and Citi Field remembered, briefly, what late-game joy sounds like. The standings will still be there in the morning. They always are.
