The Mets had built something fragile in the third inning on Thursday afternoon, and by the eighth it had come apart in their hands. MJ Melendez [2-2, 1 HR | RC 8]'s three-run home run had given them a brief lead against Washington, a moment when Citi Field allowed itself to believe, but baseball doesn't deal in sentiment. CJ Abrams [2-3, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 24] took Luke Weaver [1 IP, 2 ER, 1 K | 6.00 ERA, PAR -1.12] deep in the eighth, a two-run shot that turned the game inside out, and the Nationals walked away with a 5-4 victory that felt less like a loss than a confirmation. The game swung wildly—the WPA swing of 3.75 testified to that—but in the end it swung away from the home team, as it has so often this April.

Freddy Peralta [6 IP, 1 ER, 6 K | 3.54 ERA, PAR 2.63] deserved better. He carried the ball through six innings with precision, holding Washington to a single earned run while working around traffic, and for a time it seemed enough. But the Mets' margin for error has been almost nonexistent all month, and when the bullpen faltered, there was no cushion to absorb the blow. Juan Soto [2-3, 1 BB | RC 15] reached base three times and scored twice, doing what he could to keep the offense alive, but the lineup around him went quiet when it mattered. They are now 10-21, sitting eleven and a half games behind Atlanta in the NL East, a chasm that feels more psychological than mathematical at this point.

The division landscape only deepens the ache. Atlanta lost to Detroit, yes, but they remain 22-10, a team that can afford an off day without existential worry. Philadelphia, meanwhile, completed a doubleheader sweep of San Francisco in improbable fashion—both games decided in their final at-bat, both featuring late-inning heroics that spoke to a club still believing in its own capacity for rescue. The Phillies are 12-19, hardly thriving, but they have won three straight and carry the air of a team finding its footing. The Mets, by contrast, have now lost two in a row and seven of their last ten. Washington is right there with them at 15-17, close enough in the standings but playing with the confidence of a team that knows how to finish.

The Mets are underperforming their Pythagorean expectation, which projects them at 11.2 wins rather than ten, but even that modest uptick would leave them deep in the cellar. The offense ranks last in the division in OPS, the defense is middling, and the pitching—good on paper—keeps getting undone by single innings where everything unravels. Peralta has been a bright spot, another strong start adding to a resume that should matter more than it has, but you cannot pitch your way out of this alone. What the Mets need now is not analysis but momentum, the kind that comes from stringing together wins in bunches, from finding a way to hold a lead. They have not found it yet, and May begins tomorrow with no guarantees that they will.
