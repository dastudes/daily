There is a particular cruelty to losing in extra innings on the road, three time zones from home, after the bullpen has done its job and the lineup has scratched out just enough to believe. The Mets flew to Anaheim and played the Angels even for nine innings, then watched it slip away in the tenth — a walkoff single to center that ended a game whose 4.48 WPA swing tells you, more than any box score could, how often the lever of fate moved between dugouts before settling against New York.

Nolan McLean [4 IP, 3 ER, 6 K | 2.99 ERA, PAR 15] was not at his sharpest, but he kept the Mets in it, and the bridge work that followed — Tobias Myers [2.2 IP, 0 ER, 0 K | 2.05 ERA, PAR 8], Huascar Brazobán [1.1 IP, 0 ER, 1 K | 1.18 ERA, PAR 7], and Craig Kimbrel [1 IP, 0 ER, 1 K | 4.43 ERA, PAR 2] — held the Angels scoreless across four-plus innings of relief. That's the kind of pitching effort that ought to win a ballgame. Instead, the offense managed only flickers: a run here from Mark Vientos [1-4 | RC 8], a run there from Marcus Semien [1-4 | RC 9], Austin Slater [2-3 | RC 3] doing more with his chances than anyone else in the lineup. Bo Bichette [1-5 | RC 12] and Francisco Alvarez [1-5 | RC 11] combined for ten at-bats and almost no damage, and Juan Soto [1-4, 1 BB | RC 15]'s lone walk was the closest thing the middle of the order offered to a real threat. When you score three on the road, you generally need a pitching gem, and McLean — for all his strikeouts — wasn't quite that.

The standings, viewed plainly, are not kind. The Mets sit last in the NL East, eleven and a half games behind Atlanta, which dispatched Colorado with the kind of routine efficiency that defines runaway divisions. Chris Sale [7 IP, 1 ER, 11 K | 2.14 ERA, PAR 15] struck out eleven, Drake Baldwin [3-4, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 28] had one of those days hitters dream about, and the Braves now sit at twenty-three and ten with a run differential approaching seventy. Miami also won, blanking the Phillies behind a near-perfect outing from Max Meyer [7 IP, 0 ER, 7 K | 2.68 ERA, PAR 13]. Washington lost to Milwaukee. Philadelphia's four-game streak ended in Florida. Of the four other teams in this division, only one — Atlanta — looks like it belongs in a different conversation entirely. But the Mets are eleven and a half back of them, and four behind everyone else, which is a lonelier place to be in early [4 IP, 5 ER, 3 K | 3.84 ERA, PAR 6] May than the calendar suggests.

The Pythagorean record offers a flicker of cold comfort: at eleven and twenty-one, the Mets are actually performing slightly below what their run differential predicts, meaning the underlying numbers see them as something closer to a twelve-win team rather than a genuinely catastrophic one. The pitching staff's FIP is respectable — better than Atlanta's, in fact — and the defense has been competent. The problem, written in plain numbers across the team's OPS, is that this lineup is not hitting. A team OPS in the .630s in 2026 is the offensive equivalent of trying to fill a bathtub with a teaspoon, and it explains why nights like last night, when the pitching does enough, end the way they did.

The road trip continues, and there is plenty of season left for the math to find its level. But for now the Mets are a team whose arms are keeping them afloat and whose bats are pulling them under, and the standings reflect the difference. A win is a win — the streak, such as it is, technically shows a W1 — but Saturday night in Anaheim was the kind of loss that lingers past the cross-country flight home.
