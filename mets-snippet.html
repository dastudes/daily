Good morning. The Mets won a baseball game last night, which is the sort of sentence that has felt rarer this spring than anyone in Queens would like to admit. They beat the Angels in Anaheim, a tidy late-inning affair built on a sixth-inning rally and a solo shot from Ronny Mauricio [1-3, 1 HR | RC 2] in the seventh that gave the bullpen something to protect. Christian Scott [5 IP, 2 ER, 8 K | 4.43 ERA, PAR 1] was the foundation — five innings of the kind of work that doesn't make highlight reels but quiets a dugout — and the relay from Huascar Brazobán [1 IP, 0 ER, 1 K | 1.28 ERA, PAR 6] through Brooks Raley [1 IP, 0 ER, 1 K | 1.61 ERA, PAR 5] and Luke Weaver [1 IP, 0 ER, 2 K | 5.54 ERA, PAR 1] to Devin Williams [1 IP, 0 ER, 2 K | 7.20 ERA, PAR 1] was a small clinic in how a manager imagines his late innings going on a good night. Marcus Semien [1-4 | RC 9]'s sixth-inning single, which delivered both Juan Soto [1-3, 1 BB | RC 15] and Francisco Alvarez [1-4 | RC 11], was the swing the game turned on.

It is worth lingering, briefly, on Scott. In a season that has not given the Mets many things to point at, the young right-hander is becoming one of them — eight strikeouts on a quiet West Coast Friday, the sort of outing that travels well in memory even after the box score yellows. Mauricio, too, deserves a nod: a home run in his only productive plate appearance of the night, and the kind of contribution that rookies on losing teams sometimes get lost beneath. Last night it counted.

The trouble is the standings, which remain unmoved by single nights. The Mets are 11-21, eleven and a half games behind the Braves and in the basement of the NL East. The Pythagorean record suggests they are roughly the team their results say they are — there is no hidden good team waiting to emerge from bad luck. The run differential is ugly but not catastrophic; the team OPS, however, sits well below every rival in the division, and that is the plainest explanation for why they are where they are. They aren't hitting. They haven't been hitting. One Friday night in Anaheim doesn't change that arithmetic.

The division offered no help. Atlanta kept rolling, surviving a wild one at Coors and pushing their record to 23-10 — a team playing essentially to its expected level, which is to say, very well. Philadelphia, after a difficult April, has now won four straight; the Phillies remain under .500 but are the division's clearest mover. Miami and Washington both lost, which is the only good news available to a Mets fan studying the table this morning, and even that good news is cold: the Mets are still chasing both of them, not the other way around.

So we take what's offered. A road win, a young starter looking like a starter, a closer who closed, a rookie's home run. The season is not yet two-fifths done, and stranger climbs have happened, though it would be dishonest to pretend the shape of this team right now suggests one. For today, at least, the Mets fly home with a W, and that will have to be enough — which, in baseball, is often the only honest thing to say.
