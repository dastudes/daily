Friday Morning, May 16

The Subway Series rarely shows mercy to the loser, and last night at Citi Field the Mets discovered again that the difference between these two clubs in 2026 is not subtle. Cam Schlittler [6.2 IP, 1 ER, 9 K | 1.35 ERA, PAR 29], the Yankees' young right-hander now leading the American League in ERA, came across town and treated the Mets lineup like a road test he had already passed once before. Clay Holmes [4.1 IP, 4 ER, 8 K | 2.41 ERA, PAR 18], working against his former employers, struck out plenty but could not contain the third inning, when Ben Rice [3-5, 1 HR | RC 39], Cody Bellinger [1-5 | RC 28], and Jazz Chisholm Jr. [3-4, 1 BB | RC 16] all found the gaps. By the time Juan Soto [1-3, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 19] finally announced himself with a solo home run in the seventh, the game had long since slipped beyond reach. The total WPA swing of 1.64 tells the story plainly enough: this was not a contest that breathed late.

What lingers, more than the loss itself, is how quiet the bats were around Soto. Bo Bichette [0-4 | RC 13], Mark Vientos [0-4 | RC 14], Carson Benge [0-4 | RC 13] — the heart of what was supposed to be a deeper, more dangerous order this spring — produced nothing. Brett Baty [2-3, 1 BB | RC 13] had a useful night and Marcus Semien [1-3, 1 BB | RC 15] reached base, but the Mets are now a team whose offensive identity rests almost entirely on Soto's shoulders and the hope that one of the younger bats will catch fire. The club's team OPS is the lowest in the division by a meaningful margin. That is not a slump. That is a condition.

The standings, meanwhile, continue to harden in ways that should concern anyone planning a summer around this team. The Mets sit last in the NL East, twelve and a half games behind Atlanta, who pushed their record to 31-14. The Braves are the league's most complete team and show no sign of regression. Behind them, Washington and Philadelphia are tied at 22-23, both nine games back. Even Miami, at 20-25, sits a game and a half ahead of New York. There is no rival stumbling. There is no door opening.

The one mild consolation in the underlying numbers: the Mets' Pythagorean record suggests they have been somewhat unlucky, perhaps a game and a half worse than their run differential would project. The pitching, by FIP, has actually been quite good — better than Atlanta's, better than Washington's. The 6-4 mark over the last ten games hints that the floor is not collapsing. But baseball seasons are not won by FIP, and the offense is what it is until proven otherwise. A team with this much invested in Soto and Bichette cannot keep scoring at this rate and expect the math to forgive it.

So we arrive at mid-May with the familiar shape of a difficult season taking form: a superstar doing his part, a rotation that deserves better, and a lineup waiting for someone — Vientos, Baty, Benge, anyone — to remember what they were brought here to do. There is still time. There is always still time in May. But the Braves are not waiting, and the calendar, as ever, is the one opponent that never has a bad night.
