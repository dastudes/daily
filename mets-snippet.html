Morning Briefing: The Mets

The desert was unkind. In Phoenix, the Mets ran into Eduardo Rodriguez [8.1 IP, 1 ER, 4 K | 2.25 ERA, PAR 15] at his most economical and efficient, and the evening unraveled along familiar lines — a quiet offense, a short and costly relief appearance, and a deficit that never threatened to close. Huascar Brazobán [1 IP, 2 ER, 0 K | 2.47 ERA, PAR 6], asked to open, did not survive his inning intact, and by the time David Peterson [5 IP, 0 ER, 3 K | 5.52 ERA, PAR 7] settled in to give the Mets the long, unflashy outing they badly needed, the hole had already been dug. Luis Torrens [2-3 | RC 3] offered something to remember, and Carson Benge [1-3 | RC 10] crossed the plate on his double, but the lineup as a whole — Juan Soto [0-3, 1 BB | RC 15], Bo Bichette [0-3, 1 BB | RC 13], Marcus Semien [0-3, 1 BB | RC 12] all walking but unable to do more — never built the kind of rally that turns a deficit into a contest. The total WPA swing of 1.88 tells the story plainly: this was not a game that hung in the balance. It tilted early and stayed tilted.

The standings now read like a piece of bad news delivered in plain font. The Mets sit at 15-25, twelve and a half games behind Atlanta, riding a two-game losing streak, with the worst run differential in the division and an offense that has scored fewer runs than any of their NL East rivals. The Pythagorean projection suggests they've actually been a touch unlucky — they're underperforming their expected record by about a game — but that is cold comfort when the team above you in fourth place is nine full games ahead. The hole is not yet bottomless, but it is deep, and May is the month in which deep holes either start filling or start defining a season.

The rest of the division did the Mets no favors. Every team in the division that played yesterday won except the Mets, and the result is that New York remains alone at the bottom while the middle of the division — Washington, Philadelphia, Miami — continues to bunch tightly at nine games back, all three still within plausible reach of something if Atlanta ever blinks.

There are quieter encouragements worth noting. The Mets' team FIP of 3.52 is the best in the division, which means the pitching has, on the whole, done its job — Peterson's outing in Arizona was another data point in that direction. Sean Manaea [1 IP, 0 ER, 2 K | 6.62 ERA, PAR 1] continues to be useful in relief stretches. The problem is not on the mound; it is in a lineup whose collective OPS sits at .628, last in the division by a meaningful margin. Soto and Bichette will hit. They will. The track records are too long to suggest otherwise. But the season does not wait for regression, and the Mets are running out of room to assume that what should happen will.

A road trip that began with hope ends with the team needing to look hard at itself. The schedule offers no easy reprieve, the division leader has settled into something that looks suspiciously like a contender's groove, and the question now is less about catching Atlanta than about whether this team can become the version of itself that the projections still, faintly, believe in.
