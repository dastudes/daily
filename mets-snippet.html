Mets Morning Briefing — Thursday, May 7, 2026

A trip to Denver tends to do one of two things to a team in the doldrums: cure what ails it, or expose every wound at once. For the Mets, a third straight win — and a ten-run evening at altitude — counts as the kinder verdict. Juan Soto [1-4, 1 HR | RC 15] opened the scoring early with a homer, MJ Melendez [2-2, 1 BB | RC 10] added a triple in his abbreviated afternoon, and from there the lineup did what it has not done often this spring: pile on. The fourth inning was the hinge, a three-run burst against Michael Lorenzen [5 IP, 7 ER, 2 K | 6.92 ERA, PAR 0] that turned a tight game into a comfortable one, and the sixth-inning answer to Colorado's only real threat — when Tobias Myers [0.2 IP, 4 ER, 0 K | 3.65 ERA, PAR 5] briefly turned a five-run cushion into a one-run knife edge — felt like the kind of resilience that has been in short supply at Citi Field this year.

Marcus Semien [4-5, 1 HR | RC 12] was the centerpiece, the kind of four-hit night with a homer and three runs scored that reminds you why the Mets have been waiting on him to look like himself. Brett Baty [2-4, 1 BB | RC 9] quietly chipped in with a pair of hits and a walk, Francisco Alvarez [2-4, 1 BB | RC 13] added two hits and a walk of his own, and Carson Benge [2-5 | RC 9] kept the line moving from the bottom of the order. Freddy Peralta [5 IP, 0 ER, 1 K | 3.13 ERA, PAR 12], working five scoreless on the road, gave the bullpen a manageable bridge — though Myers nearly burned it down before Brooks Raley [1.1 IP, 0 ER, 2 K | 1.29 ERA, PAR 6] and Luke Weaver [1 IP, 0 ER, 2 K | 4.77 ERA, PAR 3] restored order. Devin Williams [0.2 IP, 0 ER, 2 K | 6.43 ERA, PAR 2] got the save, which is now a sentence Mets fans can read without flinching.

And yet the standings tell the colder story. Three straight wins and the Mets are still 14-22, still last in the NL East, still eleven games behind an Atlanta team that finally lost a game last night in Seattle. The good news, such as it is, came in pieces: the Braves dropped a quiet one to Bryan Woo [6 IP, 0 ER, 9 K | 4.02 ERA, PAR 11], the Marlins lost again at home to Baltimore (their fourth straight), and even Washington's blowout of Minnesota only moved the Nationals to 17-20 — tied with Philadelphia, eight and a half games up on New York but hardly out of reach. The Phillies, riding a four-game streak of their own, are the team in this division most clearly trending upward. The Mets are simply trending less downward, which on May 7 is something.

The peripherals offer a thin reed of hope. New York's Pythagorean record suggests they ought to be a game better than they are — not a dramatic gap, but a real one — and the team FIP of 3.53 is the second-best in the division, behind only Philadelphia. The pitching, in other words, has largely held up its end; it is the .645 team OPS, easily the worst in the East, that has buried them. Three nights of crooked numbers won't fix that, but they hint at what the offense is supposed to look like when Soto and Semien and Alvarez are all upright in the same lineup at the same time.

Worth singling out beyond yesterday: Semien now sits among the league leaders in linear weights for the day, and Sean Manaea [0.1 IP, 1 ER, 1 K | 6.92 ERA, PAR 1], despite a brief and ugly cameo last night, remains part of a rotation depth chart that, on paper, ought to keep this team in games through the summer. Eleven back is eleven back, and the Braves are not going to oblige forever. But for the first time in a while, the Mets have strung together a few days that look like baseball rather than penance. Take it.
