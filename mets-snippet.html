Mets Morning Briefing — Tuesday, May 5, 2026

There is a particular pleasure in watching a team play patient, professional baseball in a thin-aired ballpark and refuse to let the place dictate the terms. The Mets did that yesterday at Coors Field, where a scoreless game suddenly tilted in the sixth inning and never tilted back. David Peterson [4 IP, 2 ER, 6 K | 6.33 ERA, PAR 5] wore the win, though it was Austin Warren [2 IP, 0 ER, 3 K | 1.48 ERA, PAR 3] who carried the heaviest pitching share through the middle innings, and Devin Williams [1 IP, 0 ER, 1 K | 6.55 ERA, PAR 2] who closed the door. The decisive blow came from Carson Benge [1-2, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 8], whose home run — sandwiched among contributions from Mark Vientos [1-4, 2 RBI | RC 11], Luis Torrens [1-3, 1 RBI | RC 2], and Francisco Alvarez [1-3 | RC 12] — turned a quiet afternoon into a four-run inning. Juan Soto [0-3, 1 BB | RC 14] drew a walk and scored, which is, at the moment, the kind of small, useful contribution the Mets need from him while the bat works itself back into form.

It is the second straight win, and it comes in the most welcome fashion: the bullpen unblemished, the rotation answering for itself, the offense doing just enough in a single concentrated burst. A modest streak, but the Mets have not had many of those, and one takes what one is given.

The standings, however, remain a stern document. New York sits last in the NL East, eleven and a half games behind Atlanta — a gap that did narrow by a full game yesterday, since the Braves lost. The Phillies, meanwhile, won their second straight, edging Miami in a one-run game decided largely by Bryce Harper [3-4, 1 HR | RC 24] and Aaron Nola [6 IP, 0 ER, 5 K | 5.09 ERA, PAR 6]. The Marlins and Nationals are tied for second, both well clear of the Mets but both, like Philadelphia, eminently catchable if the Mets can sustain anything resembling competence over a month. The division, in other words, is functionally a two-team race between Atlanta and everyone-not-named-the-Mets, and the Mets need to climb back into that second tier before they can dream of the first.

The deeper numbers offer cold comfort and a flicker of warmth in equal measure. Their Pythagorean record suggests they have been roughly the team their run differential says they are — a touch unlucky, but not dramatically so. The pitching staff's FIP is, quietly, among the better marks in the division; the offense's OPS is the worst. That is the season in miniature: arms holding up their end, bats not yet holding up theirs. Recent form (4-6 over the last ten) reflects that imbalance more than yesterday's win obscures it.

As for individual notes worth keeping an eye on: Benge's contribution yesterday was the loudest, but Sean Manaea's rotation-mate David Peterson has now turned in another effective start in a season where Mets starters have, on the whole, been the team's most reliable feature. And Devin Williams, brought in for the ninth, looks every bit the closer the Mets paid for. The bats will need to wake up — Soto especially, Brett Baty [0-4 | RC 8] and Marcus Semien [0-3 | RC 9] too — but the infrastructure for a climb is intact. Whether the climb actually begins is the only question that matters now, and it will not be answered in May. It will only be hinted at.
