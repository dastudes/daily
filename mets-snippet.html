Mets Morning Briefing — Friday, May 8, 2026

A mile above sea level, the Mets played seven innings of credible baseball and one inning of the other kind, and that was enough to send them home with a loss they could feel in their shoulders. Through seven, this was a tight, low-scoring affair — Christian Scott [4.2 IP, 1 ER, 6 K | 3.27 ERA, PAR 1.91] had given the Mets exactly the sort of outing they needed from a young arm in a hostile park, working into the fifth with command of his strikeout pitch, and the bullpen had largely held the line behind him. The eighth inning is where the evening unraveled. Craig Kimbrel [1 IP, 4 ER, 2 K | 7.78 ERA, PAR -2.17], summoned to keep the deficit manageable, instead surrendered the sort of inning that turns a winnable road game into a four-run bottom-line beating. Colorado's Jake McCarthy [2-4, 1 HR | RC 12] did the heaviest damage, and by the time the inning closed, the game's WPA swing — nearly three full wins' worth of leverage — had tilted decisively the wrong way.

The bats, for their part, offered little to push back with. Juan Soto [1-4 | RC 16] managed a single hit in four trips. Austin Slater [2-3 | RC 3] and Tyrone Taylor [1-2 | RC 4] scratched out contact. Marcus Semien [0-2, 2 BB | RC 12] drew a pair of walks but came around only once. The shape of the night was familiar: enough baserunners to suggest something was brewing, never quite enough sequencing to make it real. Against Jose Quintana [5.2 IP, 2 ER, 2 K | 3.90 ERA, PAR 1.46] and a parade of Rockies relievers, the Mets put up two early runs and then went quiet, which at altitude is a recipe with only one outcome.

The standings tell the harder story. At 14-23, New York sits eleven and a half games behind Atlanta in the NL East — a gap that, in early May, is not yet a sentence but is no longer simply bad luck either. Their Pythagorean record suggests they're roughly the team their results indicate; a club outscored by 28 runs is, by and large, a club that loses more than it wins. The 5-5 mark over their last ten is the small mercy here: they have stopped bleeding, even if they haven't begun to heal. Around them, the division offered no relief. Washington beat Minnesota for their second straight win and now sits eight back of the Braves. Miami walked off Baltimore in the most dramatic finish of the day across all of baseball. Only Philadelphia, drubbed by the Athletics, had a worse Thursday than the Mets — small consolation, and the Phillies still sit two and a half games ahead in the loss column.

There is something quietly interesting buried in the Mets' peripherals: a team FIP of 3.56 that would, in a different universe, belong to a contender. The pitching, in aggregate, has been better than the record suggests — Clay Holmes continues to pace the National League in ERA, a fact worth holding onto on a morning like this one. The offense, at a .644 OPS, is the wound. Until the lineup catches up to what the arms are doing, nights like Thursday — competitive for seven, lost in the eighth — will keep arriving. The season is not over; it is, however, beginning to ask the Mets a serious question about who they intend to be.
