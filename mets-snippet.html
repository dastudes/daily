Mets Morning Briefing — Sunday, May 3, 2026

There is a particular cruelty to losing in extra innings on the road, and the Mets found it last night in Anaheim, dropping a one-run decision to the Angels in ten. The game itself was the most volatile of the day across all of baseball — a 4.48 WPA swing that twisted one way, then the other, before settling on the wrong side of the ledger. Nolan McLean [4 IP, 3 ER, 6 K | 2.99 ERA, PAR 15] turned in another sturdy outing, the kind that ought to win games for a healthier club, and the bullpen behind him kept the Angels off the board for nearly six innings. But a tenth-inning walkoff is a tenth-inning walkoff, and the Mets are now the kind of team that finds them.

The bats, as has been the pattern, were quiet when it mattered. Austin Slater [2-3 | RC 3] had the most productive afternoon in the box, and Tyrone Taylor [1-4 | RC 5] delivered the seventh-inning hit that briefly tied the thing. Juan Soto [1-4, 1 BB | RC 15] drew a walk and managed a hit; Bo Bichette [1-5 | RC 12] and Francisco Alvarez [1-5 | RC 11] each found a single but no more. It was the sort of offensive evening — present but not insistent — that has defined the Mets' first month. They scored, they threatened, they did not finish.

The standings tell the harder story. At 11-22, New York sits twelve and a half games behind the Braves in the NL East, last in the division and trending the wrong way on a four-and-six stretch over their last ten. Atlanta thumped Colorado on the road behind Chris Sale [7 IP, 1 ER, 11 K | 2.14 ERA, PAR 15], who was as good as anyone in the league yesterday, and Drake Baldwin [3-4, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 28], whose afternoon was the kind that wins divisions when accumulated across a summer. Miami got a near-flawless start from Max Meyer [7 IP, 0 ER, 7 K | 2.68 ERA, PAR 13] to shut out the Phillies. Milwaukee handled Washington. Every NL East team not named the Mets is closer to relevance than the Mets are, and the Braves' lead over the field has become the dominant fact of the division.

There is, if you squint, a sliver of statistical comfort: the Mets' Pythagorean record suggests they should be roughly a game better than they are — twelve wins instead of eleven, given their run differential. That is not a rescue, but it is a hint that the underlying pitching, anchored by a team FIP that is genuinely respectable, is not the problem. The problem is an OPS that ranks at the bottom of the division and a lineup that, even with Soto in it, has not yet learned to do damage in concert. Nolan McLean's emergence — he is now among the National League leaders in FIP and strikeouts — is the most encouraging individual development on the roster, and worth holding onto on a morning when there is not much else.

Twelve and a half games is not a deficit you erase in May. But it is the kind of deficit that, if ignored long enough, calcifies into something else entirely — a lost season, a roster reckoning, a summer of asking what went wrong. The Mets are not there yet. They are, however, close enough to see it from where they stand.
