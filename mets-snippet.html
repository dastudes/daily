Mets Morning Briefing — Monday, May 4, 2026

There is something instructive about a road win in Anaheim on the first Sunday in May, even when the team handing it out sits twelve and a half games back in its own division. The Mets beat the Angels in a game that never quite tightened into suspense, and the work was distributed in a way that should please anyone who has been watching this club search for an identity. Clay Holmes [6.2 IP, 1 ER, 6 K | 1.71 ERA, PAR 16], still a starter and apparently more comfortable in the role each week, gave New York the kind of outing — long, efficient, quietly authoritative — that makes a manager's late-inning decisions easier rather than harder. Behind him, Luke Weaver [1.1 IP, 0 ER, 3 K | 5.11 ERA, PAR 2] and Brooks Raley [1 IP, 0 ER, 2 K | 1.48 ERA, PAR 5] finished the thing off without incident, which is its own small mercy.

The afternoon belonged, however, to Mark Vientos [2-4, 2 HR | RC 11], whose two home runs accounted for the margin and then some. Vientos has been one of the more frustrating studies on this roster — a hitter whose talent has always seemed to arrive in pulses rather than steadily — and a day like this one, with him sitting near the top of the league's daily linear-weights leaders, is a reminder of what the Mets thought they had. Carson Benge [1-2, 2 BB | RC 7], drawing two walks and reaching base three times, did the unglamorous work of being on base when Vientos arrived. Brett Baty [1-3, 2 BB | RC 8] also walked twice. For a lineup that has too often this year looked like nine men hitting in isolation, there were at least the outlines of an offense yesterday.

None of which changes the standings much. The Mets are 12-22, last in the East, and the gap to Atlanta is the kind of distance that tends not to close so much as it gets surveyed. The Braves, who beat the Rockies in Denver in the day's most volatile game by win-probability swing, remain comfortably the class of the division and perhaps the league. Behind them, the Marlins lost to Philadelphia, the Nationals nudged past Milwaukee, and the Phillies — quietly, without fanfare — are now playing the best baseball in the division not played in Atlanta. The middle of the East is a muddle of teams between 14 and 16 wins; the Mets are the one club below that cluster, and they will need a stretch of weeks, not days, to climb back into it.

The underlying numbers offer a small consolation and a small warning at once. New York's Pythagorean record suggests they have been roughly the team their results say they are — a hair unlucky, perhaps, but not meaningfully so. The pitching, by FIP, has actually been quite good; the offense, by OPS, has been the worst in the division by a clear margin. That is the puzzle of this team in early May. The arms are doing their part. Yesterday the bats, briefly, did theirs. Whether those two things can occur on the same afternoon with any regularity is the question that will define the next month, and probably the season.

For today, though: a win in California, a flight east, and Vientos rounding the bases twice. It is not a turning point. It does not have to be. Some Sundays you simply take the result and move on, grateful the box score reads the way it does.
