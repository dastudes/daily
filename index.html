About This Graph

Run Differential

This graph shows how well each team performed scoring and allowing runs, which are the drivers of baseball success. The Runs Allowed axis (the Y axis) is in reverse, so that the best defensive teams are at the top of the graph and the best scoring teams are on the right.

The dotted lines are based on the Pythagorean Theorem (RS^2)/(RS^2+RA^2), which is a pretty good predictor of winning percentage. The lines are drawn at different winning percentages so you can compare teams in different areas of the graph. The best teams will be above the .600 line.

If you hover over a datapoint, you'll see how many actual wins that team achived. If you go back up to the Standings table, the "PythVar" is the difference between the actual wins and Pythagorean wins. Differences between Pythagorean and actual wins is typically based on random distribution patterns more than anything else.

About This Graph

The Roots of Scoring Runs

There are lots of way to break down how teams score runs. This graph is based on two key factors: getting runners on base and slugging them home. The potent combination of these two factors has led to the widespread use of OPS (On-base Average plus Slugging Percentage) as a simple but effective way to evaluate batters.

On this graph, I use Isolated Power (ISO, which is Slugging Percentage minus Batting Average) instead of Slugging Percentage because that makes the differences between teams more apparent. The best scoring teams will be in the upper right quadrant of the graph, but the X axis (OBP) tends to be more important than the Y axis (ISO). Still, this graph effectively shows different styles of run scoring between teams.

Separating the Impact of Pitching and Fielding

The easiest way to judge the effectiveness of pitching is to isolate those things that don't involve fielders: Strikeouts, Walks and Home Runs. That's what FIP ((HRA*13+BB*3-K*2)/IP+3.10) measures.

All other batting events are batted balls that involve fielders. To measure what happens on those balls, we use Defense Efficiency Ratio (DER), which is simply the percent of batted balls (not including home runs) turned into outs by fielders. DER reflects a lot of complex stuff, such the quality of the fielders, the gloves, the ballpark configurations, how hard the ball was hit, where it was hit, and probably a few more things I haven't thought of. So it isn't a perfect measure of fielding excellence but it's not bad and it's easy to calculate.

The best teams at turning batted balls into outs are on the top and the FIP axis (on the bottom) is reversed so that the best pitching teams are on the right.

Mets Morning Briefing — Saturday, May 9, 2026

Out in the desert, on a night that asked for patience and got it, the Mets finally won a game by refusing to lose one. Nine innings produced a single run for each side — Mark Vientos [2-4, 1 HR | RC 13] answering Nolan Arenado [1-4, 1 HR | RC 17]'s second-inning home run with one of his own — and then the bullpens took over, and the zeroes piled up like chairs stacked at closing time. Nolan McLean [6 IP, 1 ER, 6 K | 2.79 ERA, PAR 17] was excellent through six, and the relief corps that followed him — Luke Weaver [1 IP, 0 ER, 1 K | 4.47 ERA, PAR 3], Brooks Raley [1 IP, 0 ER, 0 K | 1.20 ERA, PAR 7], Devin Williams [1 IP, 0 ER, 1 K | 5.90 ERA, PAR 3], and Tobias Myers [1 IP, 0 ER, 2 K | 3.49 ERA, PAR 5] in the tenth — surrendered nothing. When the breakthrough came in extras, it came from Vientos again, with a double, and Carson Benge [1-3, 1 BB | RC 10] behind him with another. A 2.99 WPA swing tells you the game was tight all the way through; the box score tells you it was tighter than that.

It is a win, and wins are not to be sneered at when you are 15-23 and looking up at four other teams in your division. The Mets remain in last place in the NL East, ten and a half games behind Atlanta, but their last ten now reads 6-4, and their Pythagorean record suggests they have been roughly the team their run differential says they are — neither cursed nor blessed, just a little short. The pitching has actually been quite good; the team FIP is the best in the division. The OPS is the worst in the division by a comfortable margin. That is the whole story, more or less, and last night was a miniature of it: nine innings of fine pitching, one run of offense, and a rescue mission in the tenth.

The division did the Mets a small favor and a larger one. Atlanta lost in Los Angeles — Chris Sale [7 IP, 2 ER, 7 K | 2.20 ERA, PAR 17] pitched well enough to win most nights and didn't — which means the Braves' lead over the field stayed roughly where it was. Washington beat Miami behind a superb outing from Foster Griffin [7 IP, 1 ER, 9 K | 2.14 ERA, PAR 15], and the Nationals are now the clear second-place team, riding a three-game streak. Philadelphia, meanwhile, suffered the indignity of an extra-inning loss to Colorado after Justin Crawford [3-4, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 16]'s eighth-inning home run had seemingly turned the game. The Phillies and Marlins are tied with the Mets' nearest neighbors at 17-22. None of this changes much for New York in the immediate sense — ten and a half is ten and a half — but it does suggest a division in which only Atlanta is playing like a contender, and that is a useful thing to know in May.

A word for Vientos, who carried the offense almost single-handedly, and for McLean, whose season continues to quietly demand attention; he is among the National League leaders in both strikeouts and FIP, which is the sort of pairing that tends to mean something real. The Mets do not have many players hitting the way you'd want them to right now — the lineup last night was a parade of hitless four-at-bat lines — but they have arms, and arms keep you in games until the hitting decides to show up. It hasn't yet. There is still time.

About These Stats

These leaderboards show individual player statistics for the current season. Click any column header to sort by that stat. Click again to reverse the sort order. Use the filters above each table to focus on qualified players, specific leagues, or age groups. The "Qualified" checkbox filters for players with enough playing time to be statistically meaningful.

The stats have been pulled from the official MLB Stats API. Player names link to their Baseball Savant profiles for advanced metrics and visualizations. Lefties have an asterisk; switch-hitters have a cross.

Most of these are standard stats, but I've added a few simple sabermetric takes to sort players by their impact.

  • RC (Runs Created) is simply OBPxTB
  • FIP (Fielding Independent Pitching) ((13xHR)+(3x(BB+HBP))-(2xK))/IP + 3.10
  • PAR (Pitching Above Replacement) (6-(FIP+ERA)/2)xIP/9

These stats are value approximations only. Please don't quote them. For actual good sabermetric stats, go to Fangraphs or Baseball Reference.
