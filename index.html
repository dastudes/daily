Atlanta came into Thursday with the best record in baseball, and they were the team that had the most to lose — and lost it. The Reds, whose Pythagorean record suggests they've been outplayed by their actual record, keep finding ways to win anyway, and right now that's what counts.

Over in the AL Central, where mediocrity is a four-team negotiation, Minnesota's win over Toronto at Target Field nudged the Twins back toward the pack while pushing the Blue Jays further into AL East purgatory. The Twins, like the Braves, have been outscoring their record — their Pythagorean projection is more than two wins better than where they actually sit, which suggests this group has been on the wrong side of the breaks rather than genuinely bad. And Houston, whose pitching staff currently posts a team FIP that would embarrass a beer league, salvaged the back end of a doubleheader at Camden Yards. The Astros remain in last place in the AL West, but Lance McCullers Jr. [6 IP, 3 ER, 9 K | PAR 1.8] at least gave them a starter who looked the part.

The two games at Citizens Bank Park were the story of the day, and frankly the story of the week. The opener turned on a ninth-inning Phillies rally against San Francisco's bullpen, with Bryson Stott [1-4 | RC 8]'s triple and Justin Crawford [3-4 | RC 12]'s single walking it off after Logan Webb [7 IP, 1 ER, 6 K | PAR 2.57] had handed the lead to relief in fine shape. Then the nightcap topped it: a 6.05 WPA swing, the kind of number you don't see often, with the Giants taking the lead in the top of the ninth before Brandon Marsh [1-1 | RC 16] and Kyle Schwarber [4-4, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 24] dragged Philadelphia back into a tie, and a tenth-inning walkoff finishing it. Two games, both decided in the home half of the ninth or later, both going to the same team. If you're a Phillies fan, you remember this Thursday for a long time.

Washington's win at Citi Field deserves its own mention. CJ Abrams [2-3, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 24]'s eighth-inning homer off Luke Weaver [1 IP, 2 ER, 1 K | PAR -1.12] swung the game by nearly half a win all by itself, and the Mets — already buried in the NL East basement — dropped another one they had every chance to win. The 3.75 WPA swing tells you it was tight throughout; the standings tell you it mattered more for New York than they'd like to admit.

  • Kyle Schwarber and Milwaukee's William Contreras [4-4, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 20] tied for the top batting line of the day, both going a perfect four-for-four with a homer. Contreras did his in a laugher; Schwarber did his in a thriller.

About This Graph

Run Differential

This graph shows how well each team performed scoring and allowing runs, which are the drivers of baseball success. The Runs Allowed axis (the Y axis) is in reverse, so that the best defensive teams are at the top of the graph and the best scoring teams are on the right.

The dotted lines are based on the Pythagorean Theorem (RS^2)/(RS^2+RA^2), which is a pretty good predictor of winning percentage. The lines are drawn at different winning percentages so you can compare teams in different areas of the graph. The best teams will be above the .600 line.

If you hover over a datapoint, you'll see how many actual wins that team achived. If you go back up to the Standings table, the "PythVar" is the difference between the actual wins and Pythagorean wins. Differences between Pythagorean and actual wins is typically based on random distribution patterns more than anything else.

The Roots of Scoring Runs

There are lots of way to break down how teams score runs. This graph is based on two key factors: getting runners on base and slugging them home. The potent combination of these two factors has led to the widespread use of OPS (On-base Average plus Slugging Percentage) as a simple but effective way to evaluate batters.

On this graph, I use Isolated Power (ISO, which is Slugging Percentage minus Batting Average) instead of Slugging Percentage because that makes the differences between teams more apparent. The best scoring teams will be in the upper right quadrant of the graph, but the X axis (OBP) tends to be more important than the Y axis (ISO). Still, this graph effectively shows different styles of run scoring between teams.

Separating the Impact of Pitching and Fielding

The easiest way to judge the effectiveness of pitching is to isolate those things that don't involve fielders: Strikeouts, Walks and Home Runs. That's what FIP ((HRA*13+BB*3-K*2)/IP+3.10) measures.

All other batting events are batted balls that involve fielders. To measure what happens on those balls, we use Defense Efficiency Ratio (DER), which is simply the percent of batted balls (not including home runs) turned into outs by fielders. DER reflects a lot of complex stuff, such the quality of the fielders, the gloves, the ballpark configurations, how hard the ball was hit, where it was hit, and probably a few more things I haven't thought of. So it isn't a perfect measure of fielding excellence but it's not bad and it's easy to calculate.

The best teams at turning batted balls into outs are on the top and the FIP axis (on the bottom) is reversed so that the best pitching teams are on the right.

There is a particular cruelty to losing a game you led into the eighth, and the Mets, who have lately been finding new rooms in the house of disappointment, found another one last night at Citi Field. Freddy Peralta [6 IP, 1 ER, 6 K | 3.54 ERA, PAR 10] had given them everything a starter is asked to give — six innings of measured, intelligent work against Washington — and the lineup had pushed across enough early runs to suggest, however faintly, that this might be the night the tide turned. MJ Melendez [2-2, 1 HR | RC 7]'s third-inning home run, with Bo Bichette [0-3, 1 BB | RC 12] and Juan Soto [2-3, 1 BB | RC 15] aboard, felt like the kind of swing a slumping team builds an evening around. Soto himself reached three times. Francisco Alvarez [1-1 | RC 11] came off the bench with a hit. The shape of a win was visible.

And then Luke Weaver [1 IP, 2 ER, 1 K | 5.54 ERA, PAR 1] came on for the eighth, and the Nationals' CJ Abrams [2-3, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 24] put a ball into the right-center field seats, and the shape dissolved. The total WPA swing of 3.75 captures the volatility honestly enough, though it does not quite capture the specific texture of a bullpen letting a thin lead slip in a season where thin leads have been increasingly hard to come by. The Mets are now losers of two straight, seven of their last ten, and at 10-21 they sit eleven and a half games behind Atlanta in a division that has begun to feel less like a race than a receding horizon.

The cold comfort, if you are inclined to look for it, is that the rest of the East did the Mets no particular favors either. Atlanta lost at home to Detroit in a game that Framber Valdez [6 IP, 2 ER, 8 K | 3.37 ERA, PAR 12] essentially decided by the seventh inning, snapping the Braves' brief idle. Philadelphia, however, swept a doubleheader from San Francisco — both walk-offs, the nightcap stretching into extras — and the Phillies are suddenly playing the kind of baseball that wins close games even when their underlying numbers say they shouldn't. Washington's win pulls them within striking distance of .500. Only Miami took the day off, and they remain, somehow, six and a half games clear of New York.

The Pythagorean math is the part that should give a thoughtful Mets fan pause. The team's run differential suggests they ought to be an eleven-win club — which is to say, essentially the club they are. There is no hidden contender lurking beneath the surface, no bad-luck narrative waiting to correct itself. The pitching peripherals are respectable; the FIP is fine. The OPS, last in the division by a meaningful margin, is the honest problem. A lineup featuring Soto and Bichette and Mark Vientos [1-4 | RC 8] ought to produce more than this, and at some point the explanation has to move past sample size.

So the morning arrives with the standings unchanged in any meaningful sense and the calendar turning to May, which is when seasons begin to harden into shape. Peralta's outing was the kind of thing to hold onto — quietly excellent, the sort of start that, repeated thirty times, anchors a rotation. Soto continues to do Soto things at the plate. These are not nothing. But they are also not yet enough, and everyone at Citi Field, players and patrons alike, seems to know it.

About These Stats

These leaderboards show individual player statistics for the current season. Click any column header to sort by that stat. Click again to reverse the sort order. Use the filters above each table to focus on qualified players, specific leagues, or age groups. The "Qualified" checkbox filters for players with enough playing time to be statistically meaningful.

The stats have been pulled from the official MLB Stats API. Player names link to their Baseball Savant profiles for advanced metrics and visualizations. Lefties have an asterisk; switch-hitters have a cross.

Most of these are standard stats, but I've added a few simple sabermetric takes to sort players by their impact.

  • RC (Runs Created) is simply OBPxTB
  • FIP (Fielding Independent Pitching) ((13xHR)+(3x(BB+HBP))-(2xK))/IP + 3.10
  • PAR (Pitching Above Replacement) (6-(FIP+ERA)/2)xIP/9

These stats are value approximations only. Please don't quote them. For actual good sabermetric stats, go to Fangraphs or Baseball Reference.
