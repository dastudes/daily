The Yankees keep doing Yankee things. Their win over Baltimore was their second straight and pushes them to a comfortable lead in the AL East, with the Rays the only team in the division above water. The Orioles, meanwhile, have now dropped three in a row and sit seven back — not yet desperate, but the math is starting to tilt. Over in the NL Central, where five teams are above .500 and the standings look like a bar fight, the Cubs picked up a half-game on everyone by shutting out Arizona behind Shota Imanaga [7 IP, 0 ER, 5 K | 2.41 ERA, PAR 16]. Cincinnati's loss at Pittsburgh, a 17-7 demolition, dropped the Reds into a tie with St. Louis a game back, and the Cardinals' walk-off win over the Dodgers — their sixth straight — has turned what looked like a Cubs runaway into something genuinely interesting. The Dodgers, by the way, have now lost four in a row, and San Diego sits a half-game behind in the West despite getting shut out by the White Sox. That's the kind of weekend that resets a division.

The most dramatic game of the day, by a wide margin, was the Mets-Angels affair in Anaheim. New York's Tyrone Taylor [1-4 | RC 5] singled home the tying run in the seventh, the game crawled to extras, and Oswald Peraza [3-5 | RC 16] delivered a walk-off single in the bottom of the tenth — a 4.48 WPA swing, which is the kind of number you see when a game changes hands more than it stays put. Nearly as wild was Kansas City's win in Seattle, where Jac Caglianone [2-4 | RC 14]'s ninth-inning single off Andrés Muñoz [1 IP, 0 ER, 1 K | 5.54 ERA, PAR 2] — aided by a Julio Rodríguez [2-4 | RC 19] error — flipped what had been a Mariners win into a Royals one. That single alone was worth +0.443 in win probability. Honorable mention to Cleveland's beatdown of the Athletics, a 14-6 game whose 2.96 WPA swing speaks to how the lead changed hands before the Guardians eventually pulled away.

  • Emerson Hancock [7 IP, 1 ER, 14 K | 2.62 ERA, PAR 13] struck out 14 Royals over seven innings and lost the game anyway. Top pitching performance of the day by PAR, and his team didn't even win it. Baseball is cruel.
  • Cody Bellinger [4-4, 2 HR | RC 21] went 4-for-4 with two homers against the Orioles. The kind of day where you just hand the man the lineup card and ask if he wants to write himself in again tomorrow.
  • Chris Sale [7 IP, 1 ER, 11 K | 2.14 ERA, PAR 15] struck out 11 in seven innings at Coors Field. That deserves its own paragraph, frankly — eleven strikeouts at altitude, against any lineup, is a genuinely special outing.
  • Max Meyer [7 IP, 0 ER, 7 K | 2.68 ERA, PAR 13] allowed one hit over seven shutout innings against Philadelphia. The Phillies managed one hit total as a team. That's not a slump; that's a clinic.

The pythagorean column tells some stories worth watching. Cincinnati is now a remarkable +5.78 wins above their expected record — that's not sustainable, and getting outscored 17-7 is exactly the kind of game that starts the regression. The Dodgers, conversely, are -3.22, meaning they've been better than their record suggests, which is cold comfort during a four-game skid. Atlanta sits at 24-10 with a +76 run differential and looks like the class of the National League by a healthy margin, while the Mets, at 11-22, are quietly the worst team in baseball — and just lost in extras to an Angels club that isn't much better. Somebody has to finish last in the NL East, and right now it's pretty clearly going to be them.
