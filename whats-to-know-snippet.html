The American League East has settled into a familiar shape at the top, and Thursday did nothing to disturb it. The Yankees handled Baltimore at home behind Will Warren [6.1 IP, 1 ER, 9 K | 2.42 ERA, PAR 14], who was as efficient as anyone all day, and New York now sits comfortably ahead of Tampa Bay despite the Rays' own win over San Francisco at the Trop. The Orioles, meanwhile, slip another game under .500 and are starting to look like the team their run differential suggests they are. Out in the Central, Cleveland's wild affair in Sacramento against the Athletics — eight runs to five, with the lead changing hands more than once — pulls the Guardians back over .500 and into a tie atop a division where nobody seems particularly eager to claim the thing. The White [0-2 | RC 3] Sox, of all teams, have now won four straight after pasting San Diego at Petco, which tells you something about the state of the AL Central.

In the National League, the Cardinals' five-game winning streak rolled on at Busch [2-4 | RC 11], where they took down the Dodgers behind Matthew Liberatore [5.2 IP, 2 ER, 4 K | 4.50 ERA, PAR 3]. That's a meaningful result on two fronts: it tightens the NL Central, where St. Louis is now within striking distance of the Cubs and Reds at the top, and it snaps a Dodgers team that had looked untouchable into a third straight loss. Atlanta, the best record in baseball, kept pace by surviving Colorado on the road in a game that required a ninth-inning home run to put away. And the Phillies, who entered the day buried in the NL East basement, have now quietly won four in a row.

For pure drama, nothing topped Cleveland-Athletics, which produced the largest WPA swing of the day. The Guardians built a lead, gave most of it back in the seventh, and held on only after Cade Smith [1.1 IP, 0 ER, 1 K | 3.58 ERA, PAR 5] finished things off. Kansas City and Seattle wasn't far behind: the Royals jumped out early, Seattle clawed back on the strength of two Julio Rodríguez [2-4, 2 HR | RC 17] home runs — the second a seventh-inning shot that briefly flipped the win expectancy entirely — and then Kansas City answered in the eighth to steal it. And down at Comerica, the Rangers and Tigers traded the lead through the middle innings before Texas pushed across the deciding run in the eighth, with Alejandro Osuna [1-4 | RC 3]'s double the single biggest play of the game.

  • Shane McClanahan [6 IP, 0 ER, 5 K | 3.10 ERA, PAR 10] posted the day's top pitching mark in Tampa Bay's shutout of the Giants, walking nobody across six innings.
  • Mitch Keller [7 IP, 1 ER, 6 K | 2.85 ERA, PAR 14] was nearly as good for Pittsburgh, working into the seventh against Cincinnati in the Pirates' rout.
  • Henry Davis [2-3, 1 BB, 2 HR | RC 6] led all hitters by linear weights in that same Pirates win, with two home runs as part of a nine-run home effort.
  • Kazuma Okamoto [2-4, 1 BB, 2 HR | RC 15] hit two more for Toronto in a road win at Target Field, his second multi-homer showing of note this season.

The big-picture story [2-4 | RC 9] is the gap that's opened between Atlanta and everyone else in the National League — the Braves' run differential is now genuinely absurd, and their Pythagorean record matches their actual one almost exactly. Cincinnati, by contrast, continues to outrun its underlying numbers by more than five wins, the largest such gap in the league; that's the kind of thing that tends not to last. The Angels' seven-game losing streak has them sharing the AL West cellar with Houston, who entered the year as a presumptive contender and currently owns the worst run differential in the league. It's early, but "early" gets shorter every day.
