The most consequential game of the day was played in front of what one assumes was a quiet crowd at Citi Field, where the Nolan McLean [7 IP, 3 ER, 7 K | 2.94 ERA, PAR 18]-led Mets handled the Tigers and pulled within touching distance of nobody in particular — but Detroit, suddenly losers of three straight, has dropped into a tie with Kansas City at the bottom of the AL Central. The Royals themselves contributed to that tie by getting beaten by the White Sox at Rate Field, where Chicago has now won five in a row and pulled to within a game of Cleveland for the division lead. It's a strange Central: every team is bunched within 3.5 games, and the team that looked like it might run away with it a week ago is suddenly looking over its shoulder at the South Siders.

Out west, Seattle's win in Houston meant a one-game swing in the AL West, where the Mariners, Rangers, and Athletics are now separated by a single game at the top. Philadelphia's win at Fenway, meanwhile, was more about Boston's slide than the Phillies' chase — the Red Sox now sit 10.5 back in the East, with the Phillies pulling even with Washington for second in the NL East, both still nine games behind a Braves team that lost but only barely seems to notice these things.

The most dramatic game by a wide margin was at Sutter Health Park, where the Cardinals erased a four-run deficit to beat the Athletics in the ninth. Jordan Walker [2-5, 1 HR | RC 34]'s double in the top of the ninth and Iván Herrera [1-5 | RC 26]'s follow-up single combined for nearly 0.7 WPA between them, and the swing of nearly five full win probability points is the kind of thing you usually only see in postseason recaps. Philadelphia's win at Fenway was the slow-burn version of the same story — scoreless into the eighth before Kyle Schwarber [1-5, 1 HR | RC 36] hit his 18th of the year to put the Phillies ahead for good. And the Cubs-Braves game at Truist deserves a mention for its texture: a 2-0 shutout where Chris Sale [6 IP, 0 ER, 8 K | 1.96 ERA, PAR 21] struck out eight in six scoreless innings and lost anyway, because Ben Brown [4 IP, 0 ER, 7 K | 1.63 ERA, PAR 15] and four Cubs relievers held Atlanta to one fewer run than that.

A few performances worth pulling out:

  • Zebby Matthews [7 IP, 0 ER, 5 K | 0.00 ERA, PAR 4] of the Twins turned in the day's best pitching line, seven scoreless against Miami with only one walk allowed. Minnesota hasn't been a good team this year, but this was a good day.
  • Chris Sale struck out eight over six scoreless and got beat. Sometimes the box score is cruel.
  • JJ Bleday [3-5, 2 HR | RC 18] of the Reds had the kind of afternoon — two homers in a 15-1 rout — that briefly makes you check whether he's been doing this all season.
  • Yordan Alvarez [3-3, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 46] went 3-for-3 with a homer for Houston in a losing effort, which is roughly his job description at this point.

The NL Central remains the most interesting division in baseball, with all five teams above .500 and the Cubs holding a 2.5-game lead over both Milwaukee and St. Louis. Worth noting that San Diego's Pythagorean record (21-22) suggests the Padres have been getting away with something — they're four wins ahead of what their run differential implies, the largest such gap in baseball. The opposite is true in Milwaukee, where the Brewers' run differential says they should be the best team in the division; for now, they'll have to settle for being very good and slightly underrated.
