The cruelest losses arrive softly. In Washington, the Nationals played the Mets nearly even for nine innings, fell behind by a single run on a single inning's mischief, and then watched the bottom of the ninth unfurl in front of them like a dare — a runner in scoring position, the tying run ninety feet from home, the lineup turning over to men who had every right to believe they could deliver. They could not. Cade Cavalli [7 IP, 2 ER, 9 K | 3.89 ERA, PAR 15] had pitched the kind of game a young arm dreams of all winter, and the Nationals lost it anyway. The line between a feel-good evening and a quiet, sleepless one is sometimes nothing more than a ground ball finding a glove.

Out in Anaheim, the Angels endured something similar but worse, because there is no kinder word for what the late innings have become at Angel Stadium. The Athletics needed extras to finish them off, and the Angels, who keep finding ways to load the bases against the math, ran into a double play with the tying and winning runs aboard. They are now nine and a half games out of nowhere in particular, and their season has the unmistakable shape of a team waiting for summer to end. The Athletics, meanwhile, are tied atop the AL West with Cleveland's divisional cousins in the standings picture — a sentence that still has the texture of a typo, and yet here it is, in late May, plain as a box score.

Arizona's evening at Chase Field had the same heartbeat in reverse: a tie game broken open in the ninth by Corbin Carroll [1-5 | RC 35], the Diamondbacks' fifth straight win, and a quiet announcement that the NL West may not be a two-team conversation after all. The Rockies, who had gotten a lovely outing from Zach Agnos [5 IP, 0 ER, 4 K | 4.76 ERA, PAR 6], will fly home wondering how a game so carefully built came apart so quickly. That is the cost of playing well and losing anyway — the runs do not refund.

The day's individual brilliance was scattered generously:

  • Mike Yastrzemski [3-3, 1 HR | RC 15] of the Braves had the kind of afternoon that reminds you what his last name has always meant in a box score, contributing to Atlanta's rout of the Marlins at loanDepot park.
  • Kyle Stowers [2-4, 2 HR | RC 12], in defeat, gave Miami fans something to hold onto, twice.
  • Michael Harris II [2-5, 2 HR | RC 29] matched him from the winning side, his power surge arriving at the exact moment Atlanta has begun pulling away from the rest of the NL East.
  • Eduardo Rodriguez [7 IP, 0 ER, 4 K | 2.25 ERA, PAR 20] turned in the day's best pitching line, seven scoreless at Chase Field that the Diamondbacks nearly wasted before they didn't.
  • Luis Severino [7 IP, 2 ER, 10 K | 4.26 ERA, PAR 12] struck out ten Angels and watched his bullpen finish the job in extras.
  • Braxton Ashcraft [7 IP, 1 ER, 9 K | 2.90 ERA, PAR 20] was the quiet master of Busch Stadium, carrying the Pirates past the Cardinals with the kind of poise that makes scouts reach for second cups of coffee.
  • Brayan Rocchio [3-3 | RC 26] had a perfect afternoon at the plate in Detroit, a small but pristine line on a day when Cleveland needed every base.

The Guardians have now won six in a row, and the AL Central, which spent April pretending to be a four-team race, has begun resolving into the picture most expected: Cleveland in front, Chicago hanging on, Detroit sinking. The Tigers have lost six straight, and Casey Mize [6.2 IP, 2 ER, 4 K | 2.50 ERA, PAR 17] pitched well enough to deserve better, which is becoming the refrain of their season. Meanwhile, in the Bronx, the Yankees were shut out by a Toronto staff that used five pitchers to do it — Adam Macko [1.1 IP, 0 ER, 2 K | 0.00 ERA, PAR 2], Spencer Miles [4.1 IP, 0 ER, 6 K | 2.17 ERA, PAR 11], and Jeff Hoffman [1 IP, 0 ER, 2 K | 5.32 ERA, PAR 5] among them. The Rays keep winning. They have won four straight and eight of ten, and they are doing it without any of the marquee names the standings usually demand. There is something almost stubborn about it, as if they have decided the rest of the league simply hasn't been paying attention.

The Braves, for their part, are running. Thirty-five wins, a run differential that looks like a misprint, and a division lead before Memorial Day. The NL East was supposed to be a fight; it has become a procession. Somewhere in the middle of the standings, the Phillies and Nationals and Mets are playing perfectly respectable baseball and falling further behind anyway. That is what a great team does to a division — it makes competence look like failure. And Atlanta, on this Thursday in Miami, looked very much like a great team.
