The NL East is essentially a two-horse race that's already feeling lopsided, and Atlanta did nothing to change that at Coors Field. Chris Sale [7 IP, 1 ER, 11 K | 2.14 ERA, PAR 15] carved up Colorado's lineup over seven innings, Drake Baldwin [3-4, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 28] drove in four, and the Braves now sit three games clear of the Cubs and Reds for the best record in the National League. Meanwhile in the Bronx, the Yankees kept their grip on the AL East tightest, beating Baltimore behind a four-hit afternoon from Cody Bellinger [4-4, 2 HR | RC 21] that included two home runs. New York's plus-52 run differential is the best in the AL by a comfortable margin, and the rest of the East is now bunched between three and six games back.

Out west, the Cardinals climbed to 19-13 by knocking off the Dodgers in St. Louis, their fifth straight win and a meaningful one given how crowded the NL playoff picture is becoming — six NL teams now have at least 19 wins. Los Angeles, despite losing three in a row, still owns a Pythagorean record that suggests they should be running away with the West rather than sharing it with San Diego. And the White [1-3 | RC 3] Sox — yes, that White Sox — won their fourth in a row, blanking the Padres at Petco behind Sean Burke [6 IP, 0 ER, 8 K | 2.76 ERA, PAR 13]. Chicago is still under .500 and still has a lousy run differential, but they've quietly played the best baseball in the AL Central over the last ten games.

The day's most dramatic finish came in Anaheim, where the Mets and Angels combined for a 4.48 WPA swing before Oswald Peraza [3-5 | RC 16] singled home the winning run in the tenth. The Angels had lost seven straight coming in; getting walked off into a win is a small mercy for a team that's now nine games under. Almost as wild was Seattle, where the Royals and Mariners traded zeroes for most of the night — Emerson Hancock [7 IP, 1 ER, 14 K | 2.62 ERA, PAR 13] struck out fourteen — before Kansas City's Jac Caglianone [2-4 | RC 14] broke through in the ninth with the help of a Julio Rodríguez [2-4 | RC 19] fielding error, then added an insurance run in the tenth. Hancock deserved better than a no-decision; he was the best pitcher in baseball today and his bullpen couldn't hold a one-run lead.

A few performances worth singling out:

  • Emerson Hancock of the Mariners: 14 strikeouts in seven innings of one-run ball, the day's best pitching line by PAR. The loss is on the bullpen and the defense, not him.
  • Cody Bellinger of the Yankees: a perfect day at the plate with two home runs, and the top LWTS figure of any hitter in the majors.
  • Chris Sale of the Braves: eleven strikeouts in seven innings at Coors Field, which is the kind of thing you don't see often enough to take for granted.
  • Max Meyer [7 IP, 0 ER, 7 K | 2.68 ERA, PAR 13] of the Marlins: a one-hit shutout effort over seven innings against Philadelphia, the kind of start that quietly puts a struggling team back in the win column.

The Reds remain the most interesting Pythagorean story [1-4 | RC 10] in baseball — they're 20-12 with a negative run differential, and their Pythagorean projection has them at 14.7 wins, more than five games below where they actually sit. They lost ugly at PNC Park, getting blasted 17-7 by Pittsburgh, which is the sort of result that starts dragging the actual record toward what the underlying numbers suggest. On the other end, Milwaukee continues to underperform its run differential, and Houston, despite a strong offensive showing today behind Christian Walker [3-4, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 27] and Brice Matthews [2-3, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 8], is now 12-21 with a pitching staff posting a 5.24 FIP. That's not bad luck. That's a problem.
