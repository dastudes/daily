Saturday in baseball gave us two extra-inning thrillers, a couple of statement games at the top, and one absolutely dominant pitching line that deserves to be framed.

Start with the AL East, where the Yankees and Rays keep playing pitch-and-catch at the top. New York handled Baltimore at home behind a four-hit afternoon from Cody Bellinger [4-4, 2 HR | RC 21], and Tampa Bay matched them by dispatching San Francisco at the Trop. The gap stays at a game and a half, and with the Blue Jays now six back and the Red Sox nine back, this division increasingly looks like a two-team affair. Meanwhile in the NL Central — the only division in baseball with five teams above .500 — the Cardinals kept their winning streak rolling against the Dodgers at Busch on the strength of Jordan Walker [2-4, 1 HR | RC 28]'s third-inning home run, while the Cubs quietly stretched their own streak by shutting out Arizona at Wrigley behind Shota Imanaga [7 IP, 0 ER, 5 K | 2.41 ERA, PAR 16]. Chicago now leads Cincinnati and St. Louis by a game; nobody can afford a slip. And in the NL West, the Padres' shutout loss to the White Sox at Petco — a fourth straight defeat — has shaved their deficit behind the Dodgers down to half a game even though Los Angeles also lost. That's the kind of week where treading water counts as progress.

The drama trophy goes to Anaheim, where the Mets and Angels combined for a 4.48 WPA swing in extras. Los Angeles' Oswald Peraza [3-5 | RC 16] delivered the walkoff single off Austin Warren [0.2 IP, 0 ER, 0 K | 2.20 ERA, PAR 1] in the tenth, capping a game in which the Mets had clawed back to tie in the seventh and then watched Bo Bichette [1-5 | RC 12] ground into a back-breaking double play in the top of the tenth. Not far behind in tension was Seattle, where the Royals broke a tie in the ninth on Jac Caglianone [2-4 | RC 14]'s single — a play that became a triple thanks to Julio Rodríguez [2-4 | RC 19]'s error in center — and then survived the home tenth as Lucas Erceg [1 IP, 0 ER, 2 K | 3.83 ERA, PAR 4] struck out the side's worth of trouble. Honorable mention to Cleveland-Athletics, a 2.96 WPA swing that was less about late drama and more about the Guardians simply refusing to stop hitting; they put up runs in seven different innings on the way to a 14-6 win.

  • Emerson Hancock [7 IP, 1 ER, 14 K | 2.62 ERA, PAR 13] of the Mariners turned in the day's best pitching performance and one of the more remarkable lines you'll see this year — fourteen strikeouts, no walks, one earned run over seven innings. That his team lost in extras is one of baseball's small cruelties.
  • Chris Sale [7 IP, 1 ER, 11 K | 2.14 ERA, PAR 15] struck out eleven over seven for the Braves on the road in Colorado, which is a sentence I never quite get used to typing.
  • Cody Bellinger went a perfect 4-for-4 with two home runs in the Yankees' win over Baltimore.
  • Max Meyer [7 IP, 0 ER, 7 K | 2.68 ERA, PAR 13] allowed one hit over seven shutout innings as the Marlins blanked Philadelphia.

The Pythagorean column continues to flag the Reds as the league's biggest overachiever — they've outpaced their run differential by nearly six wins, and a 17-7 loss at PNC isn't going to help close that gap. On the other side, the Dodgers keep underperforming their expected record by more than three wins and have now dropped four straight, which is the kind of thing that solves itself in May but doesn't feel that way when you're watching it. And spare a thought for the Giants, losers of five in a row and now sporting the worst OPS in the National League; the offense that was supposed to come together around Devers [1-4 | RC 10] and Chapman [0-4 | RC 14] simply hasn't.
