The pennant races, this last day of May, were shaped less by the leaders than by the pursuers — those teams clinging to the edge of contention, where every game in late spring already carries the faint metallic taste of October. Consider the Phillies and Dodgers at Chavez Ravine, a meeting of contenders that hung on a single swing. Philadelphia's Edmundo Sosa [1-2, 1 HR | RC 8], a reserve infielder more accustomed to filling lineup gaps than tilting them, supplied the moment that wrenched the game away from Los Angeles in the eighth. The Dodgers, who have spent the season as the National League's most relentlessly competent team, lost a half-game in a wild card chase that doesn't concern them and a full game of cushion in a division they still control comfortably. For the Phillies, fighting to keep pace with Atlanta's runaway Braves, it was the kind of win — close, late, on the road, against a rival — that a team remembers in September.

Atlanta itself rolled into Cincinnati and quietly extended the gap on everyone behind them. Ronald Acuña Jr. [2-4, 1 BB, 2 HR | RC 26] had the kind of afternoon that reminds you why his name still sits atop scouting reports across the league, and Matt Olson [1-3, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 44] and Jorge Mateo [1-4, 1 HR] added the punctuation. The Reds, who entered the day clinging to the bottom rung of the wild card ladder, slipped a half-game further from relevance. Meanwhile in Houston, the Astros put together their most complete game in some time against the division-leading Brewers, Jeremy Peña [3-5, 1 HR | RC 12] orchestrating things from the leadoff spot. Milwaukee's lead in the NL Central remains intact, but the Astros — buried in a brutal AL West — took back a small piece of the wild card conversation they had seemed ready to abandon.

The day's two cardiac arrests came in Texas and Baltimore. At Globe Life Field, the Royals built a lead, surrendered it, took it back, and then watched Jake Burger [3-5 | RC 27] end matters with a single off Lucas Erceg [0 IP, 3 ER, 0 K | 6.40 ERA, PAR 2] in the ninth — the kind of loss that lingers in a clubhouse already running out of reasons for optimism. The Orioles' comeback against Toronto was stranger still, a ninth inning that unspooled through walks and a Cowser [1-2 | RC 10] double until Adley Rutschman [0-3, 2 BB | RC 24] drew a free pass with the bases loaded to send everyone home. Baltimore has been a disappointment all year, but a win like this — patient, unhurried, almost accidental — is the sort of thing that hints at a team remembering what it used to be.

Elsewhere, the Pirates and Twins traded haymakers at PNC Park in a game neither pitching staff seemed eager to claim, Jake Mangum [3-4, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 11] delivering the decisive blow for Pittsburgh in a five-team NL Central where every club now sits above .500 — a small, strange miracle of parity. The Mets quietly stacked a third straight win behind Christian Scott [5 IP, 1 ER, 8 K | 2.99 ERA, PAR 10], the Nationals overwhelmed San Diego in a seventh inning that produced six runs and an unraveling of the Padres' bullpen, and the White Sox, of all teams, made it four in a row by handling Detroit with something approaching ease. None of it changes the shape of the standings dramatically. But the season has reached the point where shape begins to matter — where the teams that are who they thought they were begin to separate from those who are not. Today, a few more lines came into focus.
