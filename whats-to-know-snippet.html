The NL East is essentially a one-team show with Atlanta eight games clear, but the chase for second — and respectability — got interesting. The Nationals hung thirteen on Baltimore, the Phillies shut out Pittsburgh behind a performance we'll get to shortly, and the Mets took the Subway Series opener from the Yankees. Three NL East teams tied at .500 (well, Washington and Philadelphia tied; the Mets are climbing toward them) makes for the kind of muddled middle that produces interesting Julys. The Yankees, meanwhile, have lost seven of ten and are watching Tampa Bay pull away in a division they were favored to win.

In the AL Central, where everyone seems determined to stay within shouting distance of mediocrity, Cleveland's win over Cincinnati and the White Sox' thumping of the crosstown Cubs kept the top two locked at one game apart. Detroit and Minnesota both lost, which means the Tigers and Twins remain tied for the basement of the contending tier, four and a half back. And out west, Houston beat Texas to climb back toward the pack — though five games out with this roster's underlying numbers feels like a long road.

The most dramatic game of the day, by a comfortable margin, was Miami's ten-run, ten-inning win at Tropicana Field. The Marlins' Javier Sanoja [2-5 | RC 10] doubled home the go-ahead run in the top of the ninth, the Rays answered with a Nick Fortes [1-2 | RC 12] single to tie it in the bottom half — those two plays alone accounted for nearly eight tenths of a win in WPA swings — and then Miami simply broke the thing open with an eight-spot in the tenth against Hunter Bigge [1 IP, 7 ER, 0 K | 6.11 ERA, PAR 1]. A genuinely strange game.

Boston's win in Atlanta belongs in the same conversation. The Red Sox' Payton Tolle [8 IP, 2 ER, 3 K | 2.09 ERA, PAR 11] and the Braves' Bryce Elder [8 IP, 3 ER, 3 K | 2.03 ERA, PAR 23] matched zeros and near-zeros into the eighth, when Willson Contreras [2-4, 1 HR | RC 25] lifted a two-run homer off Elder that swung the game by nearly half a win in a single swing. Aroldis Chapman [1 IP, 0 ER, 0 K | 0.59 ERA, PAR 8] then walked the tying run to third in the ninth before Ha-Seong Kim [0-4 | RC 0] grounded out to end it. Toronto-Detroit also deserves a mention: ten innings, two runs total, decided by a Daulton Varsho [2-3, 1 BB | RC 20] single in the top of the tenth.

A few performances worth singling out:

  • Cristopher Sánchez [9 IP, 0 ER, 13 K | 1.83 ERA, PAR 29] of the Phillies threw a complete-game shutout with thirteen strikeouts and no walks at PNC Park. That's a 5.89 PAR, and it's not close to anything else anyone did on the mound today. He now shares the NL strikeout lead and stands alone atop the NL in ERA, FIP, and PAR. This was an ace's afternoon.
  • San Francisco's Casey Schmitt [4-5, 2 HR | RC 26] went 4-for-5 with two home runs against the Athletics, posting the day's best linear weights total.
  • The White Sox' Munetaka Murakami [2-3, 1 BB, 2 HR | RC 33] hit two more homers against the Cubs and now leads the American League in that category.
  • Washington's Keibert Ruiz [3-5, 1 HR | RC 10] drove in five in the Nationals' rout of Baltimore, including a three-run shot in the second that essentially decided the game before most fans had settled in.

The Pythagorean ledger continues to flag a few teams worth watching. San Diego is four wins ahead of its expected record, St. Louis nearly four, Cincinnati nearly four — all teams whose run differentials suggest the standings may not hold. On the other side, the Dodgers (whose 15-2 demolition of the Angels won't help their pythag look any more reasonable) are four and a half wins *below* expectation, and the Brewers, who keep grinding out one-run wins like today's at Target Field, two and a half below. The NL Central, where all five teams are above .500, remains the most genuinely competitive division in baseball — and probably the most fun.
