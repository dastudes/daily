Out in Anaheim, on a Wednesday night that ran past anyone's bedtime, the Athletics and Angels played the kind of game that tests a fan's patience and rewards their loyalty in equal measure. The lead changed hands like a passed note, extra innings arrived as if summoned, and when it was over Oakland — or rather, the Athletics, who exist now in a kind of geographical limbo that mirrors their place in the standings — had nudged themselves another half-step ahead of Texas in the American League West. The Angels, meanwhile, who began the day already gazing up at the rest of the division from considerable depth, lost ground they could ill afford to lose. Jeff McNeil [1-4, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 19]'s ninth-inning home run forced the matter; Tyler Soderstrom [2-5 | RC 18] settled it in the tenth. The win was the Athletics' second in a row, modest by the standards of contenders but precious in a division where modest is enough.

The afternoon's other extra-inning affair, at Comerica Park, carried a different sort of weight. Cleveland has now won five straight, and Detroit has lost five straight, and somewhere in the middle of that the AL Central has come into clearer focus. The Guardians' Tanner Bibee [8 IP, 1 ER, 5 K | 3.75 ERA, PAR 14] was magnificent across eight innings, the kind of outing that gets remembered in October if October cooperates, and Angel Martínez [1-5 | RC 24]'s tenth-inning triple was the blow that finally broke a game that had refused to break for nine. Detroit slips further; Cleveland's lead grows; José Ramírez [1-5 | RC 29] continues to play in the background of every Guardians win, which is its own kind of compliment.

Up at Tropicana Field, Tampa Bay did what Tampa Bay has been doing all spring — finding a way. The Rays trailed Baltimore into the eighth and emerged from the eighth ahead, Jonathan Aranda [1-3, 1 BB | RC 28]'s double the pivot point of the evening. They've now won four straight and sit at eighteen games over .500 in May, an absurd thing to type about a team that plays in a dome with catwalks. The Yankees, four games back, lost a tight one to Toronto in which Trey Yesavage [6 IP, 0 ER, 8 K | 1.08 ERA, PAR 13] carved through their lineup for six innings and Cam Schlittler [6 IP, 2 ER, 7 K | 1.50 ERA, PAR 32] pitched well enough to win on most nights. This was not most nights. The Blue Jays, still buried in the East, take what they can get; the Yankees, for the first time in a while, look like a team that has begun to wonder.

In the National League, the standings tell a story of compression and divergence at once. The NL Central has five teams above .500 — every team in the division, in fact — and yet Milwaukee, behind Kyle Harrison [7 IP, 0 ER, 11 K | 1.79 ERA, PAR 19]'s eleven-strikeout afternoon at Wrigley, keeps pulling slightly away. The Cubs have now lost five straight, which is the kind of streak that reorders a clubhouse's understanding of itself. Pittsburgh shut out St. Louis in Busch Stadium, Konnor Griffin [4-5 | RC 21] providing the kind of four-hit afternoon that scouts file away for later. Cincinnati battered Philadelphia behind a nine-run road performance highlighted by Sal Stewart [4-5, 1 HR | RC 34], whose evening was the sort that, were it to recur with any frequency, would change the way Reds lineups are constructed.

Out west, the Dodgers and Braves continue their parallel coronations. Los Angeles shut out San Diego at Petco, Shohei Ohtani [1-4, 1 BB, 1 HR] doing what Shohei Ohtani does — homering and pitching five scoreless in the same game, a sentence that should require a footnote and yet by now does not. Atlanta hammered Miami behind Chris Sale [7 IP, 1 ER, 8 K | 1.89 ERA, PAR 25], who is quietly assembling another Cy Young case while the rest of the league argues about Schlittler and Sánchez [2-2, 1 BB | RC 19]. The Braves now sit eighteen games over .500. The Phillies and Nationals are tied for second in the East, nine back, which is another way of saying the race is over before Memorial Day.

  • Chris Sale (Atlanta) — seven innings of one-run ball, the kind of start that no longer surprises anyone

  • Konnor Griffin (Pittsburgh) — four hits at Busch Stadium for a young Pirate worth watching

The shape of the leagues, in late May, is beginning to set. Atlanta and Tampa Bay are running, the Dodgers and Brewers behind them, Cleveland establishing itself as the answer to the Central's open question. The wild-card races are where the drama lives now — Cincinnati and Arizona and Washington and Pittsburgh all gained ground today, a four-team scrum that will likely become a six- or seven-team scrum by July. There is still a great deal of season left. But the teams that need to be playing well are playing well, and the teams that needed something to change today — the Angels, the Tigers, the Cubs in their own quiet free-fall — got more of the same.
