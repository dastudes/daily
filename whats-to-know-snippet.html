The most consequential game of the day might have been the quietest one. Atlanta's romp at Dodger Stadium was effectively a no-doubter from the second inning on, but the Braves now sit at 28-13 and have pushed every other NL East team to nine games back or worse. Meanwhile in the AL East, Tampa Bay's win at Fenway over Boston nudged the Rays back into sole possession of first, with the Yankees losing in Milwaukee on Brice Turang [2-4, 1 HR | RC 28]'s walkoff homer — a two-game swing in the race that's the only one in baseball with two teams that look like they belong.

The NL Central remains the league's most crowded picture, and the Brewers' walkoff win over the Yankees moved them to within 3.5 games of the first-place Cubs, who lost to Texas. All five NL Central teams are above .500, which is the sort of standings oddity you'd ordinarily expect to correct itself, except that the Reds beat Houston behind Andrew Abbott [6 IP, 0 ER, 5 K | 4.49 ERA, PAR 8] and now sit five back at 22-19. The NL West tightened too, with Arizona handling the Mets and San Francisco pulling out a twelve-inning thriller against Pittsburgh that cost the Pirates a wild card game; the Dodgers and Padres remain tied atop the division.

That Pirates-Giants game was the day's drama leader by a comfortable margin, with a WPA swing nearly double the next-closest contest. Pittsburgh scored two in the top of the tenth on Spencer Horwitz [2-5, 1 BB | RC 18]'s double; San Francisco answered with two in the bottom on Willy Adames [3-6 | RC 13]' two-run single; the Pirates threatened in the eleventh and twelfth and came up empty both times before the Giants finally walked it off. Nick Castellanos [1-2, 1 HR | RC 6]' two-run homer in the bottom of the ninth against St. Louis was the single most valuable swing of the day by WPA, turning a likely Cardinals win into a tie that San Diego eventually closed out in the tenth. And the Brewers-Yankees walkoff deserves its own mention — Aaron Judge [1-3, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 38] had already homered, Carlos Rodón [4.1 IP, 3 ER, 4 K | 6.59 ERA, PAR 0] had walked five but somehow allowed only three runs, and then Brice Turang ended it with one swing against David Bednar [0.2 IP, 1 ER, 2 K | 3.71 ERA, PAR 6] in the ninth.

  • Jacob deGrom [7 IP, 0 ER, 10 K | 2.65 ERA, PAR 16] was deGrom, shutting out the Cubs over seven innings with ten strikeouts and no walks at Globe Life Field. The top PAR of the day, and it wasn't particularly close.
  • Cristopher Sánchez [7 IP, 0 ER, 7 K | 2.12 ERA, PAR 24] matched him with a seven-inning shutout effort of his own against Colorado, walking nobody. He now leads the NL in PAR.
  • Kyle Schwarber [3-4, 2 HR | RC 33] and Jo Adell [3-4, 2 HR | RC 21] each went 3-for-4 with two home runs, tying for the day's LWTS lead. Schwarber's second homer pulled him into a tie with Aaron Judge for the major-league lead.
  • Eduardo Rodriguez [8.1 IP, 1 ER, 4 K | 2.25 ERA, PAR 15] went into the ninth for Arizona, allowing one run on four hits — the kind of start the Diamondbacks badly need more of.

Atlanta's underlying numbers — a +87 run differential, a Pythagorean record of nearly 30 wins — suggest the Braves are the best team in baseball, and they're starting to play like it. The Reds, on the other hand, are an interesting study: they've won two in a row to climb to 22-19, but their Pythagorean record is 16.8, the largest positive gap in the majors. Cincinnati is outperforming its run differential by more than five wins, which is the kind of thing that tends not to last. On the other side of that ledger, the Dodgers are the league's biggest Pythagorean underachiever at nearly four wins below expectation; they've lost two straight and should be running away with the NL West rather than sharing first with San Diego.
