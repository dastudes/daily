There is a particular cruelty to the way the National League East has arranged itself this May, and the Braves, who arrived at loanDepot park as the league's best team, were reminded of it on Monday. Atlanta's loss to the Marlins was not merely a defeat but a humiliation — the kind of game that sends a manager to the showers wondering what, precisely, the matter is. Miami's Max Meyer [6 IP, 0 ER, 6 K | 2.88 ERA, PAR 18] worked six scoreless innings with the assurance of a man who had been waiting for this assignment, while the Atlanta bullpen, summoned to clean up after JR Ritchie [4 IP, 6 ER, 4 K | 5.00 ERA, PAR 2], only spread the mess wider. The Braves still lead their division by seven games over Philadelphia, which is to say their cushion remains thick, but for one night the cushion sat untested while the standings beneath them rearranged in quiet ways. The Phillies, behind a tidy outing from Andrew Painter [6 IP, 2 ER, 3 K | 5.83 ERA, PAR 4] and a late home run from Bryson Stott [1-3, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 16], have now won five straight. The Mets, scuffling for weeks, found ten runs in a single extra inning in Washington and have suddenly won three in a row. Even the Marlins, ten games back and presumably playing out the string of their own modest expectations, contributed to the day's collective verdict: the Braves are excellent, but they are not running away.

Out west, the Padres did to the Dodgers what only the Padres seem able to do — beat them at the precise pitch of intensity the rivalry demands. Michael King [7 IP, 0 ER, 9 K | 2.32 ERA, PAR 20] and Yoshinobu Yamamoto [7 IP, 1 ER, 8 K | 3.32 ERA, PAR 15] traded zeros for seven innings apiece, the kind of pitching duel that ought to be filed away and revisited in October, and in the end the game turned on a single swing from Miguel Andujar [2-4, 1 HR | RC 21]. San Diego has now won four straight and holds first place in the West by half a game, a margin that feels appropriate for two teams who have spent the season's first quarter measuring each other with calipers. The Dodgers' run differential remains absurd, their Pythagorean record suggests they ought to be running away with things, and yet here they are, looking up.

The most theatrical game of the night belonged to the Angels, who entered the ninth inning trailing the Athletics by a run and exited it celebrating at home plate. J.T. Ginn [8 IP, 2 ER, 10 K | 2.99 ERA, PAR 14] had pitched eight innings of two-hit, ten-strikeout baseball for Oakland, the sort of performance that, on most evenings, sends a starting pitcher home with a win and a quiet drive through the freeway dark. Instead, Ginn watched the Angels' bullpen — having matched him almost stride for stride — hand the game to Zach Neto [1-3, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 26], whose two-run homer off Chase Silseth [0.2 IP, 0 ER, 0 K | 2.37 ERA, PAR 4]'s counterpart in the ninth ended things in the most abrupt manner the sport allows. There is no consolation in a no-decision after eight innings of work that good, only the small private knowledge that you did your job. The Angels remain last in the West. The win matters less than its texture.

In Washington, the Mets and Nationals played eleven innings of something resembling baseball, and then the Mets played a twelfth of something else entirely — ten runs in the twelfth, a number that arrives in a box score like a misprint. The game swung 6.10 points of win probability before it ended, the largest such swing of the day, and one suspects neither bullpen will sleep well. The Mets have crept back toward respectability without anyone quite noticing; the Nationals, who had played the visitors even for eleven innings, were left to absorb the kind of loss that lingers.

Elsewhere, the day's standout individual performances:

  • Josh Bell [3-4, 2 HR | RC 17] of the Twins led all hitters by linear weights, his two-homer afternoon powering Minnesota past Houston and pulling the Twins back into the wild card conversation.
  • Justin Foscue [3-3, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 5] of the Rangers had three hits including a home run in a losing effort at Coors Field, where Texas surrendered a late lead to Colorado.
  • José Ramírez [3-4, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 28] continued his quiet excellence with three hits and a home run as Cleveland buried Detroit at Comerica Park, extending the Guardians' lead in the AL Central to two games over the White Sox and pushing the Tigers to a third straight loss.
  • Junior Caminero [2-4, 2 BB, 1 HR | RC 31] homered and drove in four for Tampa Bay in the Rays' sixteen-run evening against Baltimore, the kind of game that confirms what the standings already say: the Rays are the class of the American League East, by three games over the Yankees and by a chasm over everyone else.
  • Michael King, J.T. Ginn, Bryan Woo [6 IP, 0 ER, 8 K | 3.51 ERA, PAR 17], and Yoshinobu Yamamoto all delivered starts that would headline most nights; that they share the leaderboard with one another is the sort of coincidence the schedule occasionally provides.

The American League Central, meanwhile, continues to be the division that nobody wants to win and nobody quite manages to lose. Cleveland sits in first by two games; the White Sox, blanked into the late innings by Bryan Woo in Seattle, fell back; the Twins gained; the Tigers and Royals both dropped further from the pack. It is a four-team race with no clear favorite and an asterisk hovering over every result. In the NL Central, where four teams now stand above .500, the picture is the opposite — a logjam of competence, with the Cubs and Brewers separated by half a game and the Cardinals breathing on both. Milwaukee has won eight of ten. The Cubs have lost three straight. The order, like everything else this May, feels provisional.
