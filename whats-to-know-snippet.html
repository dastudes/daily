There were fifteen games on Sunday, and a few of them actually mattered for the standings. The Rangers' shutout of Houston at Daikin Park is the obvious place to start — Texas pulled within a game of the Athletics in the AL West while pushing the Astros five back, and Nathan Eovaldi [7 IP, 0 ER, 8 K | 3.65 ERA, PAR 12] made it look easy. In the NL East, Atlanta's thumping of Boston keeps the Braves at a comfortable eight games clear of Philadelphia, though the Phillies did their part by blanking Pittsburgh behind Zack Wheeler [7 IP, 0 ER, 8 K | 2.02 ERA, PAR 13]. The interleague Subway Series finale went the Mets' way in ten innings, which matters less for the Yankees in the AL East — they're still three back of Tampa Bay after the Rays handled Miami — than it does as a small tug in the crowded NL wild card picture, where the Mets, Diamondbacks, Giants and Phillies all gained ground on a day when most of their competition lost. Arizona's win at Coors over Colorado was straightforward enough; Mickey Moniak [0-4, 1 BB | RC 32]'s Rockies are now eleven back in the NL West and going the wrong way.

The drama, though, was on the South Side. The Cubs–White Sox game at Rate Field produced a 5.27 WPA swing, which is the kind of number you get when both teams refuse to lose. The White Sox built a lead, gave it up when Michael Conforto [2-4, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 16] hit a three-run shot in the ninth to put the Cubs ahead, then Edgar Quero [3-5, 1 HR | RC 5] answered with a walkoff two-run homer in the tenth. That's the kind of game you remember regardless of what either team's record looks like in October. The other obvious one is the Yankees–Mets affair at Citi Field, where Tyrone Taylor [1-3, 1 HR | RC 5]'s three-run homer in the bottom of the ninth tied it and the Mets walked it off an inning later — a 4.07 WPA swing and the kind of loss that lingers for a New York bullpen that had it in hand. The Brewers–Twins game in Minneapolis deserves a mention too; Milwaukee scored in the top of the ninth to tie it, only for Bailey Ober [5 IP, 3 ER, 1 K | 3.63 ERA, PAR 13] to come away with the win after the Twins finished things off.

  • Zack Wheeler was the best pitcher on the board Sunday, his seven shutout innings at PNC Park outdueling Paul Skenes [5 IP, 5 ER, 7 K | 2.62 ERA, PAR 21] in what was effectively a no-contest after the fifth.
  • Gavin Sheets [3-3, 2 BB, 2 HR | RC 24] of the Padres put up the day's top batting line at T-Mobile Park — two home runs in a three-hit afternoon, and the highest LWTS of any hitter in baseball.
  • Gunnar Henderson [4-5, 1 HR | RC 22]'s four-hit, one-homer day at Nationals Park was the kind of performance the Orioles have been waiting on; whether it changes anything for a team ten games out of first is another matter.
  • Roki Sasaki [7 IP, 1 ER, 8 K | 5.15 ERA, PAR 3] struck out eight Angels over seven innings at Angel Stadium, walking nobody, which is roughly what the Dodgers paid for.

The opposite story is unfolding in Tampa Bay, where the Rays sit atop the AL East despite a Pythagorean projection that says they should be closer to .500; the Yankees, by run differential, are the better team in that division and it isn't close. And spare a thought for the Angels, who've now lost six straight and sit seven and a half back in a division where nobody is above .500 — that takes some doing.
