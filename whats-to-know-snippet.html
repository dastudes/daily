The most consequential game of the day, in pennant-race terms, was probably the quietest. Mitch Keller [7 IP, 1 ER, 6 K | 2.85 ERA, PAR 14] and the Pirates dismantled the Reds at PNC Park, and because Cincinnati and Pittsburgh occupy adjacent rungs in the NL Central ladder behind the Cubs, that's a full game's worth of separation that wasn't there the day before. Speaking of the Cubs, they outlasted Arizona at Wrigley in a game that got considerably more interesting in the sixth inning than Chicago would have liked, but a win is a win, and the Cubs now sit tied with Atlanta — actually, Atlanta still leads the NL by winning percentage. Over in the AL East, the Yankees kept rolling against Baltimore behind Will Warren [6.1 IP, 1 ER, 9 K | 2.42 ERA, PAR 14], extending what has quietly become the longest hot streak in baseball — they're now ten games clear of .500 and have built one of the largest run differentials in the league. And in St. Louis, the Cardinals took down the Dodgers behind Jordan Walker [4-4 | RC 26] and Matthew Liberatore [5.2 IP, 2 ER, 4 K | 4.50 ERA, PAR 3], which is their fifth straight win and pulls them into the mix near the top of the Senior Circuit standings.

For pure drama, nothing beat what Cleveland and the Athletics did to each other in Sacramento. The 4.27 WPA swing was the largest of the day, with the Guardians scoring in five different innings and the A's twice clawing their way back into it before Cleveland finally pulled away. Kansas City's win in Seattle had its own fireworks, with Julio Rodríguez [2-4, 2 HR | RC 17] of the Mariners providing the single most impactful play of any game — a seventh-inning two-run shot that flipped the win expectancy chart on its head — only for the Royals to answer in the eighth and steal it back. And the Phillies-Marlins game in Miami deserves a mention as well: Philadelphia took a four-run seventh inning lead, watched Miami score three in the eighth, then survived a ninth-inning rally that ended with the tying run on base.

A few performances to note:

  • Henry Davis [2-3, 1 BB, 2 HR | RC 6] of the Pirates had the day's biggest LWTS total, with two home runs in three at-bats as part of Pittsburgh's rout of Cincinnati.
  • Kazuma Okamoto [2-4, 1 BB, 2 HR | RC 15] went deep twice for Toronto in their win at Target Field, both times to left field.
  • Shane McClanahan [6 IP, 0 ER, 5 K | 3.10 ERA, PAR 10] turned in the day's best pitching line by PAR, walking nobody in six scoreless innings as Tampa Bay shut out the Giants.
  • Will Warren struck out nine in just over six innings against Baltimore, the highest strikeout total of any starter on the day.

The bigger picture: the Yankees are running away with things in the AL East and have a Pythagorean expectation of 22 wins, matched by the Dodgers in the NL. The Braves and Cubs are doing similar work in the National League, and Atlanta's plus-68 run differential is the largest in the sport. The teams to watch in the other direction are the Reds, who keep winning despite a negative run differential — their Pythagorean record suggests they should be more than five games worse than they are — and the Angels, who have now lost seven straight and are tied with Houston for the worst record in the American League. The Astros, for their part, are the most curious case in baseball: a team scoring runs at a top-five clip but allowing them faster than anyone else.
