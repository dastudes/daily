The Chicago Cubs have not won a baseball game since the Coolidge administration — or so it feels at Clark and Addison, where ten straight losses have transformed a perfectly respectable ballclub into a roadside attraction. Tuesday's contribution to the freak show came at PNC Park, where Pittsburgh's Braxton Ashcraft [6.1 IP, 1 ER, 5 K | 2.77 ERA, PAR 23] carved them up like Sunday brisket and Esmerlyn Valdez [1-4, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 2] put a first-inning homer into the Allegheny. The Cubs are now tied with the Pirates — the *Pirates*, mind you — in the NL Central standings, which is the baseball equivalent of waking up to find your accountant has moved into your guest room. Milwaukee, meanwhile, just keeps purring. The Brewers' Kyle Harrison [6 IP, 0 ER, 2 K | 1.58 ERA, PAR 23] painted six scoreless on the Cardinals at American Family Field, the bullpen finished the shutout, and St. Louis went home with the kind of box score you fold up and pretend you never saw.

Out west, the Dodgers continue to behave like a team that ordered the rest of the division off the menu. Colorado got nine innings of batting practice at Chavez Ravine, with Mookie Betts [3-5, 2 HR | RC 9] and Andy Pages [4-5, 1 HR | RC 38] taking turns auditioning for the cover of next year's media guide and Enrique Hernández [2-2, 1 HR | RC 9] chipping in just because. San Diego, the only team within shouting distance, picked a bad night to lose at home — Philadelphia's Aaron Nola [6 IP, 2 ER, 5 K | 5.77 ERA, PAR 6] strolled through Petco like a man checking his mail, Bryce Harper [1-2, 2 BB, 1 HR | RC 38] and J.T. Realmuto [2-3, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 11] went deep, and Trea Turner [2-5, 1 HR | RC 21] added another. The Padres are now three and a half back and trending toward four. Arizona, riding a hot streak that nobody outside Phoenix has noticed, beat the Giants at Oracle behind Ketel Marte [1-5, 1 HR | RC 32]'s tiebreaking seventh-inning shot — that's nine wins in their last ten, which is the sort of run that gets a manager a contract extension and a parking space near the door.

The day's drama, though, belonged to two places nobody picked in spring training. At Comerica Park, the Angels were minding their own business, losing politely, until the eighth inning, when Vaughn Grissom [3-5, 1 HR | RC 12] — yes, that Vaughn Grissom — hit a grand slam that turned the game inside out. Detroit, which has now lost nine of ten and looks every bit of it, watched the lead vanish like a wallet in a magic show. And at Rate Field, the Twins and White Sox played eleven innings of something resembling baseball: Minnesota's Joe Ryan [7.2 IP, 2 ER, 9 K | 2.95 ERA, PAR 23] was brilliant, the White Sox's Sean Burke [7 IP, 2 ER, 8 K | 3.90 ERA, PAR 16] matched him, and Munetaka Murakami [1-5, 1 HR | RC 39] tied it in the eighth with his nineteenth homer of the year — still leading the American League, in case you've been napping. Then Brooks Lee [2-5 | RC 24] doubled home three in the eleventh and that was that. Murakami also grounded into an unassisted double play in the tenth, which is the kind of thing that happens to you when the baseball gods are bored.
