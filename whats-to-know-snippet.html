Out West, Misery Loves Company

The most consequential game on the slate may have been the quietest. In Seattle, the Mariners and Diamondbacks went ten innings before Victor Robles [1-1 | RC 3] lined a walk-off single to score Randy Arozarena [1-5 | RC 37], giving the Mariners a one-run win that pushed their streak to six and dropped Arizona to a third straight loss. Seattle's starter, Bryce Miller [5 IP, 0 ER, 6 K | 1.71 ERA, PAR 9], was excellent over five hitless-but-for-one innings, and Luis Castillo [5 IP, 1 ER, 3 K | 5.55 ERA, PAR 7] matched him with five of relief work to pick up the win. The Mariners are now alone atop the AL West — not by much, but the field behind them is doing them favors. The Athletics lost their fourth straight type of game in which they couldn't hold a lead, the Astros were shut out by Jacob Misiorowski [7 IP, 0 ER, 8 K | 1.65 ERA, PAR 34] and the Brewers (more on that in a moment), and the Angels dropped another at Tropicana Field. Seattle didn't have to be brilliant to gain ground today. They were anyway.

The Misiorowski performance deserves its own paragraph. Milwaukee's young right-hander was untouchable in Houston, working seven scoreless against an Astros lineup that, while diminished without the production it expected from Yordan Alvarez [0-3, 1 BB | RC 57] today, is still a major-league offense. The only run he needed came from Jake Bauers [1-4, 1 HR | RC 30], who took Tatsuya Imai [6 IP, 2 ER, 5 K | 5.57 ERA, PAR 1] deep in the fourth for a two-run shot that accounted for the entire scoring. Misiorowski's PAR for the day was the best in baseball, and his season numbers — already among the National League's elite — keep getting harder to argue with. The Brewers continue to look like the class of the NL Central, and given that all five teams in that division are now above .500, that's not faint praise.

Elsewhere in the pennant-race conversation:

  • Kyle Bradish [7 IP, 0 ER, 4 K | 3.46 ERA, PAR 16] of the Orioles threw seven shutout innings against Toronto before the bullpen made it interesting; Colton Cowser [2-3, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 12]'s three-run homer in the third was the day's signature blow at Camden Yards.
  • Jack Leiter [5.2 IP, 0 ER, 10 K | 4.36 ERA, PAR 13] struck out ten in under six innings as Texas beat Kansas City, with Ezequiel Duran [1-4 | RC 26]'s first-inning triple doing most of the early damage.
  • Yoshinobu Yamamoto [5.1 IP, 0 ER, 10 K | 2.87 ERA, PAR 21] also fanned ten, in his case over 5⅓ innings, as the Dodgers cruised past Philadelphia. Max Muncy [2-4, 1 HR | RC 33] and Alex Freeland [2-3, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 12] both homered.
  • Braxton Ashcraft [6 IP, 2 ER, 11 K | 2.79 ERA, PAR 25] punched out eleven Twins in six innings at PNC Park, where the Pirates kept their improbable little hot streak going.
  • Nolan McLean [5 IP, 1 ER, 2 K | 4.22 ERA, PAR 15] walked five but allowed just one run over five innings as the Mets pounded the Marlins, with Marcus Semien [2-2, 2 BB, 1 HR | RC 20] providing the day's top linear-weights performance.

The day's most dramatic finish by WPA swing was that Arizona-Seattle extra-innings affair, but Boston's comeback at Progressive Field wasn't far behind. The Red Sox trailed Cleveland heading to the seventh before erupting for six runs, the rally keyed by a Masataka Yoshida [2-5 | RC 14] two-run single that produced the single biggest WPA swing of the day across the majors. Ranger Suarez [5 IP, 4 ER, 10 K | 3.40 ERA, PAR 18] got the no-decision despite striking out ten — he gave up four runs in five innings, which is the kind of line that tells you everything and nothing — and the Boston bullpen, anchored by Jovani Morán [1.1 IP, 0 ER, 1 K | 3.19 ERA, PAR 8] and finished by Aroldis Chapman [1 IP, 0 ER, 2 K | 0.49 ERA, PAR 10], didn't allow a thing the rest of the way. Boston is still buried in the AL East, but they're now within shouting distance of the wild-card conversation. Cleveland, meanwhile, has now lost two in a row with the White Sox — yes, the White Sox — breathing down their neck in the Central. Colson Montgomery [1-3, 1 HR | RC 35]'s seventh-inning home run off Drew Anderson [0.2 IP, 2 ER, 0 K | 4.03 ERA, PAR 9] was the difference in Chicago's fifth straight win, and the South Side is suddenly one game out of first place. I'm not sure anyone had that on their bingo card.

A word on the Coors Field game, because someone has to say it: the Giants put up nineteen runs and it still felt routine. Jung Hoo Lee [5-6 | RC 29] had five hits, Rafael Devers [4-6, 1 BB | RC 30] had four, Bryce Eldridge [4-6, 1 HR | RC 9] had four with a homer, Willy Adames [3-5, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 28] drove in four — and Colorado's pitching staff surrendered the whole mess with the kind of resignation you'd expect from a team now sixteen-and-a-half games out and sporting the worst run differential in the National League. The Rockies are bad in a way that's starting to feel structural rather than circumstantial. San Francisco isn't much better in the standings, but they at least got to enjoy themselves for an afternoon, which at this point in their season counts as a win in more ways than one.
