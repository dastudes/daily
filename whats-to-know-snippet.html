Down in Houston, the Astros got the kind of pitching performance they desperately needed and the kind of result that suddenly makes their position in the AL West a little less hopeless. Spencer Arrighetti [7.1 IP, 0 ER, 5 K | 1.50 ERA, PAR 14] carved up Texas over seven-plus, and Jack Leiter [7 IP, 1 ER, 6 K | 4.39 ERA, PAR 9] matched him for the Rangers nearly stride for stride before yielding the difference-making solo shot to Isaac Paredes [1-3, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 20]. Houston is still six back in the division, but they pulled within striking distance of the wild-card conversation, and Texas — two back of the Athletics — took a step in the wrong direction. Speaking of Seattle, the Mariners now sit two games behind the A's after Randy Vásquez [6 IP, 0 ER, 3 K | 2.69 ERA, PAR 17] and three San Diego relievers spun a four-hit shutout at T-Mobile Park, with Mason Miller [1.1 IP, 0 ER, 4 K | 0.86 ERA, PAR 13] slamming the door. The AL West is the most cramped race in baseball, and three of its four contenders lost yesterday.

Over at Comerica, Detroit climbed out of the AL Central basement (well, tied for it, anyway) with a walkoff against Toronto, Spencer Torkelson [1-3, 1 BB | RC 18] delivering the single off Jeff Hoffman [0.2 IP, 1 ER, 2 K | 6.13 ERA, PAR 3] that scored Matt Vierling [1-4 | RC 10]. The Tigers had lost eight of ten coming in; this is the sort of result that either starts a thaw or simply delays the inevitable. And in the NL East, where Atlanta has effectively rendered the division a one-team show, the Braves needed ten innings and a Mike Yastrzemski [1-2 | RC 11] double off Tyler Samaniego [0 IP, 0 ER, 0 K | 1.19 ERA, PAR 6] to dispose of Boston. The Red Sox now sit at the bottom of the AL East.

That Royals-Cardinals affair in St. Louis was the day's drama queen, an 11-inning, 5.99-WPA-swing thing that ended on a Yohel Pozo [1-1 | RC 2] pinch single. The Cardinals trailed, tied it, fell behind in the tenth on a Bobby Witt Jr. [2-5 | RC 34] double, retied it on an Alec Burleson [2-5 | RC 26] single, then walked it off in the eleventh. Kansas City has now dropped five straight, which is the sort of streak that turns a 19-26 record into a serious problem. The Tigers walkoff and the Braves walkoff both belong in the conversation too, and the Phillies-Pirates ten-inning slugfest at PNC — where Philadelphia eventually broke through with a three-run ninth and three more in the tenth keyed by Brandon Marsh [1-5 | RC 26]'s double — was its own kind of memorable, if only because both bullpens spent the evening trading body blows.

  • Cedric Mullins [4-4, 1 HR | RC 8] went 4-for-4 with a homer for Tampa Bay, the kind of line that doesn't need explanation.

A glance at the standings produces one number that screams louder than the rest: San Diego is 26-18 with a run differential of minus-one, which gives the Padres a Pythagorean record more than four games worse than their actual mark. That's the largest overperformance in the majors, and history suggests it won't last — though sitting a half-game behind the Dodgers in the NL West, they're not going to apologize for it. On the flip side, the Pirates' Pythag says they should be a game-plus better than 24-21, which makes their loss to Philadelphia sting a little extra. The NL Central, meanwhile, has four teams over .500, which is either a sign of unusual depth or unusual mediocrity, depending on whether you're a glass-half-full type. I'll let you decide.
