The most consequential game of the night, in terms of races that actually matter, was probably Bryce Harper [3-4, 1 BB, 2 HR | RC 29]'s personal demolition of the Athletics in Philadelphia. The Phillies have crawled back to within shouting distance of the National League wild card picture, and beating Oakland by eight runs while Cristopher Sánchez [8 IP, 0 ER, 10 K | 2.43 ERA, PAR 19] turned in the kind of start that tends to win Cy Young votes pushed them another game closer. Less encouragingly for the rest of the NL East, the Braves' walkoff-resistance act in Seattle keeps Atlanta nine and a half clear of a four-way tie for second — Washington, Philadelphia, and Miami all sit at 16-20, which is the sort of standings line that suggests the division was decided in April.

Out west, the Padres and Astros both did real damage. San Diego's beatdown of the Giants pulled them within half a game of the Dodgers, who lost in Houston when Shohei Ohtani [7 IP, 2 ER, 8 K | 0.97 ERA, PAR 18] was outpitched — a sentence I did not expect to type — by Houston's Peter Lambert [7 IP, 0 ER, 4 K | 2.44 ERA, PAR 8]. The Astros are still buried at 15-22, but they handed Los Angeles a loss that lets San Diego breathe down their neck. And in the AL Central, where five teams sit within a game and a half of first, Kansas City's win over Cleveland flipped the Royals past the Guardians in the loss column and into a virtual tie for the division lead. That's what passes for a pennant race in that division: everyone clinging to the rail at .500.

For sheer drama, the Cubs and Reds played the game of the night. Cincinnati led into the eighth before Chicago's Michael Busch [3-4, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 16] tied it with a homer, and then Busch — again — singled home Dansby Swanson [1-5 | RC 15] in the bottom of the tenth for the walkoff. The Cubs have now won seven straight. The Orioles-Marlins slugfest came in a close second on the WPA meter, with Baltimore scoring twice in the ninth on Adley Rutschman [1-1 | RC 14]'s pinch-hit single to break a tie that Miami had built largely on the back of Liam Hicks [4-5, 1 HR | RC 23]. And the Rays scored two in the eighth to flip a game they had no business winning against Toronto, the kind of late inning that quietly costs a team like the Blue Jays a season.

  • Cristopher Sánchez was the night's best pitcher and it wasn't particularly close — eight innings, no runs, ten strikeouts against an Athletics lineup that had no answers.
  • Bryce Harper carried Philadelphia's offense almost by himself, with two home runs accounting for most of the damage in a nine-run win.
  • Eduardo Rodriguez [7 IP, 0 ER, 7 K | 2.53 ERA, PAR 11] shut out the Pirates over seven, which combined with Arizona's five-run sixth inning made for a thoroughly one-sided night at Chase Field.
  • Mike Trout [2-2, 2 BB, 1 HR | RC 31] reached base in all four trips, including a homer — a reminder that when he's healthy, he's still Mike Trout.

The standings continue to suggest a few teams are living above their means and a few below. Cincinnati's Pythagorean record sits nearly five games below their actual record, and they've now lost five straight — regression has a way of arriving on schedule. Tampa Bay is similarly four games ahead of where their run differential says they should be. On the other side, Milwaukee's Pythag says they should be three and a half games better than 18-16, and the Dodgers' minus-67 run differential suggests they're a better team than their record. The NL Central remains the only division in baseball where every team is above .500, which is either a testament to balance or an indictment of the schedule. Probably some of both.
