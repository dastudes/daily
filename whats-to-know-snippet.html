There is something almost cruel about a Sunday in the South Side. The Cubs and White Sox, separated by eight miles and a generally agreed-upon understanding that one of them is supposed to be the city's serious franchise, met at Rate Field and produced a game that ran ten innings and accumulated the largest WPA swing of the day across the major leagues. The Cubs led. The Cubs led again. The Cubs led a third time, in the ninth, on Michael Conforto [2-4, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 16]'s drive to center, and then Edgar Quero [3-5, 1 HR | RC 5], who had been doing damage all afternoon, ended it in the tenth. For the Cubs, who arrived in first place and leave still in first place, the loss is the kind that gnaws — not because it changes anything in the standings (Milwaukee also lost, after all) but because of how it was lost, with two leads coughed back in consecutive innings. The White Sox, meanwhile, are now a game out in the AL Central and showing every indication that they intend to be a nuisance.

The other extra-inning affair was played across the boroughs, where the Mets beat the Yankees in ten on Tyrone Taylor [1-3, 1 HR | RC 5]'s ninth-inning home run to tie and a tenth-inning sequence that turned on a double play. The Mets remain mired near the bottom of the NL East — eleven and a half games behind Atlanta, a deficit that has the look of permanence — but a win over the Yankees in May is its own currency, redeemable for goodwill and the temporary suspension of disquiet. For the Yankees, this was the second straight loss, and with Tampa Bay winning again at the Trop behind Drew Rasmussen [5.1 IP, 2 ER, 2 K | 3.19 ERA, PAR 14], the gap in the AL East has widened to three. The Rays' lead is no longer a curiosity. It is becoming the season's organizing fact in that division.

The day belonged, in its quieter way, to starting pitchers. Zack Wheeler [7 IP, 0 ER, 8 K | 2.02 ERA, PAR 13] of the Phillies took apart the Pirates at PNC Park and outdueled Paul Skenes [5 IP, 5 ER, 7 K | 2.62 ERA, PAR 21], which is no small thing — Skenes remains among the most fearsome young arms in the league, and Wheeler simply was better. Texas's Nathan Eovaldi [7 IP, 0 ER, 8 K | 3.65 ERA, PAR 12] authored a shutout effort against the Astros at Daikin Park, deepening Houston's slide; the Astros now sit five back in a division where no one is above .500, a curious purgatory in which the Athletics, blown out by the Giants behind Adrian Houser [6 IP, 1 ER, 3 K | 5.25 ERA, PAR 3], somehow remain in first. The AL West this year resembles less a pennant race than a waiting room. Someone will eventually be called. No one seems eager.

Roki Sasaki [7 IP, 1 ER, 8 K | 5.15 ERA, PAR 3] continued to look like the pitcher Los Angeles imagined when they signed him, working through seven against the Angels as the Dodgers won their fifth straight and pulled even with the Cubs for the best record in the National League. The Padres, behind two home runs from Gavin Sheets [3-3, 2 BB, 2 HR | RC 24] — whose linear-weights line was the day's best at any position — kept pace a half-game back. The NL West has tightened into a two-team affair, with Arizona's win at Coors Field (Corbin Carroll [2-4, 1 BB, 2 HR | RC 32] with two homers of his own) doing little to alter the basic shape: Los Angeles and San Diego, and then a considerable drop. Atlanta, meanwhile, dispatched Boston behind Grant Holmes [6 IP, 0 ER, 4 K | 3.82 ERA, PAR 9] and Austin Riley [2-4, 1 HR | RC 20]'s first-inning homer, and the Braves' lead in the NL East now stands at nine. Nothing about that division feels contested anymore.

A few smaller pleasures: Gunnar Henderson [4-5, 1 HR | RC 22] had the kind of afternoon at Nationals Park that reminds you why Baltimore once seemed inevitable, even as the Orioles remain ten back. Kevin Gausman [6 IP, 0 ER, 5 K | 3.47 ERA, PAR 17] went about his business in Detroit with characteristic economy. Salvador Perez [2-2, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 15], who has been catching baseballs in Kansas City for what feels like the duration of a presidential term, hit a home run that proved sufficient — Royals 2, Cardinals 0, Stephen Kolek [6.1 IP, 0 ER, 3 K | 4.24 ERA, PAR 3] and the bullpen doing the rest. And Kyle Manzardo [2-5, 2 HR | RC 14] hit two for Cleveland, who took sole possession of first in the AL Central as Chicago's late-night theatrics played out a few hundred miles away. The Guardians have not been spectacular. They have simply been there, every day, which in a division like this one turns out to be enough.
