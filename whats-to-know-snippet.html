The most consequential game of the night was probably in Baltimore, where the Orioles knocked off the Yankees and dragged the AL East race in a useful direction — for the Rays, anyway. New York had been quietly stalking Tampa Bay, but a loss combined with the Rays' win in Toronto pushes the deficit back to two games and extends what is now a four-game Yankees losing streak. The Blue Jays, meanwhile, gave up ground they couldn't afford to give in the same division, and a Toronto club already nine and a half back is starting to look like it's playing for next year.

Out west, the AL West got tighter in the way only the AL West can — by everyone losing. Seattle's win in Houston pulled the Mariners to within two of the Athletics, while the Angels and Astros continue their joint exploration of the division basement. Arizona's 1-0 win in Texas, on the other hand, made for the rare night when a Diamondbacks victory mattered more for the loser; the Rangers slipped further behind in a division where Texas can ill afford to lose ground to anyone, let alone a sub-.500 club from the other league's West. And the Giants beat the Dodgers in Los Angeles, which mostly helps San Diego — the Padres now sit alone in first by half a game, with the Dodgers' three-game losing streak doing the work for them.

The Giants-Dodgers game produced the biggest WPA swing of the night, and it earned it honestly — San Francisco scored in four different innings, including a two-run sixth and a three-run seventh that turned a close game into a rout. Max Muncy [2-4, 1 HR | RC 28]'s home run for the Dodgers in the sixth was the single biggest play by win probability, and then the Giants immediately answered with back-to-back rallies that gutted the L.A. bullpen. The Soroka shutout effort in Arlington was a different kind of drama — a 1-0 game decided in the first inning and then defended for eight more, with every late Texas baserunner threatening to undo it. And in Baltimore, Coby Mayo [1-2, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 7]'s three-run homer in the seventh off Brent Headrick [0.2 IP, 1 ER, 1 K | 2.12 ERA, PAR 8] flipped a 2-0 Yankees lead into a 3-2 deficit in a single swing, the kind of half-inning that decides series.

Atlanta and the Cubs continue to lap their respective divisions, and the NL Central remains the only division in baseball where five teams are above .500 — a logjam that won't last but is fun while it does. Worth noting too that the Reds are a Pythagorean curiosity, sitting at 22-19 with a run differential of minus-33; that's the sort of gap that historically closes from the wrong end.
