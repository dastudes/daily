Toronto went down to Camden Yards and stole one in the eighth, and that's the game that mattered most today. Baltimore's Chris Bassitt [6 IP, 1 ER, 2 K | 5.08 ERA, PAR 7] gave them six innings of the kind of pitching you build a season around — pounding the zone, letting his defense work. The Orioles had a one-run lead and a chance to claw back toward the East. Then the bullpen got involved. Anthony Nunez [1 IP, 1 ER, 2 K | 5.15 ERA, PAR 4] walked the bases loaded and walked in the tying run on a pitch that got challenged and confirmed. Toronto's Patrick Corbin [5 IP, 1 ER, 4 K | 3.67 ERA, PAR 12] matched Bassitt pitch for pitch through five, and Jeff Hoffman [1 IP, 0 ER, 2 K | 4.66 ERA, PAR 7] and Louis Varland [1.1 IP, 0 ER, 0 K | 0.30 ERA, PAR 17] nailed it down. The Jays pick up a half-game; Baltimore drops another in a division they're already ten back in. You hand a one-run lead to a reliever, the strike zone better be his friend. Today it wasn't.

Out in Arlington, Houston's Spencer Arrighetti [6 IP, 1 ER, 3 K | 1.34 ERA, PAR 17] did what good starters used to do — went six, gave up one run, kept his pitch count reasonable, and handed it off. Four relievers finished the last three innings, which is more than I'd have used, but the game was decided in the first inning anyway. Isaac Paredes [1-3, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 24] and Jeremy Peña [2-4, 1 HR | RC 10] both went deep in the top of the first off Nathan Eovaldi [7 IP, 5 ER, 6 K | 3.96 ERA, PAR 13], and that was the ballgame. Eovaldi pitched seven, by the way — a complete-game effort by today's standards. Rangers fall further behind in a West where nobody's above .500 and Houston quietly crept back into the picture.

At Rate Field, the White Sox keep doing something nobody expected. Davis Martin [6 IP, 1 ER, 5 K | 2.01 ERA, PAR 29] outpitched Simeon Woods Richardson [2.2 IP, 5 ER, 4 K | 7.82 ERA, PAR -5] and now sits among the league leaders in ERA and PAR for the season. I had Martin in a Single-A start years ago and wrote down "competitor." That still applies. The big inning was a third-inning rally capped by Randal Grichuk [1-4 | RC 10]'s bases-clearing double — three runs scored on a ball in play, which is how you used to win games before everyone decided the only acceptable hit was one that lands in the seats. Chicago closes within striking distance of Cleveland in the Central.
