The NL Central is starting to look like the most interesting division in baseball, even if the Cubs keep playing like they don't want to give it up. Chicago lost in Texas, and behind them, the Cardinals, Brewers, Pirates, and Reds all have winning records. Milwaukee's walkoff over the Yankees pulled them within a half-game of St. Louis, who fell to San Diego, while Pittsburgh's rout in San Francisco and Cincinnati's win over Houston tightened things further. Five teams above .500 in one division — that's not a race, that's a scrum.

Out west, the Padres kept pace with the Dodgers, who lost at home to Atlanta. San Diego is now a single game back of Los Angeles, and given that the Dodgers' Pythagorean record suggests they should be running away with things, the fact that Manny Machado [1-4, 1 HR | RC 15] and Ty France [1-3, 1 HR | RC 11] are keeping San Diego close is a real story. Meanwhile in the AL Central — a division where nobody seems to want first place — Cleveland's extra-inning loss to Minnesota dropped the Guardians' lead to 1.5 games, with Kansas City surging behind a Michael Wacha [7 IP, 0 ER, 6 K | 3.63 ERA, PAR 16] gem. The White Sox also won, meaning four of the five teams in that division are within 2.5 games of the top.

The Twins-Guardians game went eleven innings and produced the largest WPA swing of the day, with Byron Buxton [2-5, 1 HR | RC 28]'s go-ahead double in the eleventh finally breaking through against a Cleveland pitching staff that had held Minnesota to one run through ten. Tanner Bibee [6 IP, 1 ER, 9 K | 2.24 ERA, PAR 9] was magnificent for Cleveland and got nothing for it. The Yankees-Brewers game was nearly as wild — New York scored in the top of the tenth on a Ryan McMahon [1-1 | RC 11] single, only to watch Milwaukee's Jackson Chourio [1-5 | RC 5] walk it off in the bottom half. And the Nationals-Marlins game in Miami featured a Washington blown lead, a Marlins comeback capped by Jakob Marsee [1-3, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 12]'s three-run shot in the eighth, and a frantic three-run Nationals ninth that came up just short.

A few performances deserve their own line:

  • Ernie Clement [5-5, 1 HR | RC 21] of the Blue Jays went 5-for-5 with a homer in Toronto's 14-1 demolition of the Angels. That is, technically speaking, as well as one can hit.
  • Alec Bohm [3-4, 2 HR | RC 9] of the Phillies hit two home runs off Colorado pitching at Citizens Bank Park.
  • Spencer Strider [6 IP, 0 ER, 8 K | 3.52 ERA, PAR 3] of the Braves struck out eight Dodgers in six shutout innings at Dodger Stadium, which is the kind of line that reminds you why he was Spencer Strider in the first place.
  • Michael Wacha posted the day's best PAR with seven shutout innings in Kansas City's win over Detroit.

The Pythagorean column is starting to whisper some interesting things. Cincinnati is outperforming its run differential by more than five wins — the largest gap in the majors — and the Reds have a minus-38 differential despite sitting at .525. That is not sustainable, and the underlying numbers (a team FIP near five) suggest the Reds are the team most likely to fall back in that crowded NL Central. On the other side, Milwaukee is underperforming by more than three wins despite the best team FIP in the National League, which means the Brewers are probably better than fourth place suggests. The Braves and Cubs sit tied atop their respective standings, but Atlanta's run differential is the best in baseball — they look like the team to beat, even if the Cubs have matched them win for win so far.
