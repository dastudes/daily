There are nights in May when the standings seem to shrug and reset themselves, when the contenders and pretenders briefly trade clothes in the dark. This was one of them. Around the league, the teams expected to be running away were caught looking over their shoulders, while a few clubs you'd written off in April quietly added another line to their case.

Begin in Anaheim, where the Athletics and Angels played the day's longest and tightest game, ten innings of mounting unease at a half-empty Big A. The Angels have been losing for a while now — three in a row, nine of their last ten — and this loss carried a particular kind of cruelty, the kind where you spent an extra inning in the on-deck circle preparing for a moment that never came. Oakland, improbably, holds first place in the AL West. It is a slim and crooked first place, with a record barely above .500 and a run differential in the red, but a division title doesn't ask how you got there. The Angels, meanwhile, slip further from any honest reckoning of contention, and Mike Trout [1-3, 1 BB | RC 34]'s afternoon — a hit and a walk in a one-run loss — feels like one more entry in the long ledger of his career's misallocated brilliance.

The day's other walkoff belonged to Arizona, where Corbin Carroll [1-5 | RC 35] of the Diamondbacks ended things in the ninth against Colorado. Five wins in a row now for the Diamondbacks, who have crept back into the periphery of the NL West conversation behind the Dodgers and Padres. The Rockies, for their part, have lost three straight and remain anchored to last place; their young left-hander Zach Agnos [5 IP, 0 ER, 4 K | 4.76 ERA, PAR 6] pitched beautifully in a losing cause, which is becoming a familiar Colorado sentence. In Washington, the Mets held on to a slender lead through a ninth inning that nearly came apart at the seams — Daylen Lile [2-4 | RC 29]'s double put the tying run in scoring position before Devin Williams [1 IP, 0 ER, 1 K | 4.44 ERA, PAR 5] finally closed the door. New York has been quietly playing well, and the Nationals, who entered the day three games ahead of the Mets in the East, now find themselves chased by a team they thought they'd left behind.

Toronto's shutout of the Yankees at the Stadium was perhaps the most quietly satisfying result of the night for anyone tired of the AL East feeling preordained. The Blue Jays remain well back, but a quiet bullpen game against Carlos Rodón [5 IP, 1 ER, 7 K | 4.15 ERA, PAR 3] and the Yankee lineup is the kind of evening that suggests this team still has something to say. The Yankees, meanwhile, have lost two straight and slipped further behind Tampa Bay, who continue to play improbable baseball — outscoring their Pythagorean expectation by four and a half wins and refusing to let the conversation get reasonable. In the Central, Cleveland made it six wins in a row, and Detroit, having lost six in a row, is officially in the part of the calendar where the schedule stops being a friend.

Some individual notes from a night that produced a number of them:

  • Eduardo Rodriguez [7 IP, 0 ER, 4 K | 2.25 ERA, PAR 20] of the Diamondbacks gave Arizona seven innings of shutout work, the day's top pitching line by PAR, and walked away with a no-decision because that is sometimes how it goes.
  • Luis Severino [7 IP, 2 ER, 10 K | 4.26 ERA, PAR 12], pitching for the Athletics in Anaheim, struck out ten across seven innings of two-run baseball — his second straight strong outing in a rotation that needs him.
  • Braxton Ashcraft [7 IP, 1 ER, 9 K | 2.90 ERA, PAR 20] of the Pirates carved up the Cardinals in St. Louis, the kind of start that makes a team that's been hovering around .500 wonder if it might be something more.
  • Cade Cavalli [7 IP, 2 ER, 9 K | 3.89 ERA, PAR 15] of the Nationals matched the moment with seven innings of his own and got nothing for it but a loss, his offense unable to solve David Peterson [5 IP, 1 ER, 3 K | 5.05 ERA, PAR 10].
  • Mike Yastrzemski [3-3, 1 HR | RC 15] of the Braves and Miami's Kyle Stowers [2-4, 2 HR | RC 12] posted the day's two highest batting linear weights, on opposite sides of a Braves rout in Miami; Michael Harris II [2-5, 2 HR | RC 29] of Atlanta wasn't far behind.
  • Brayan Rocchio [3-3 | RC 26] of the Guardians had a perfect day at the plate as Cleveland's winning streak rolled on.
  • Ernie Clement [3-4 | RC 26] of the Blue Jays put together the kind of multi-hit afternoon that doesn't make headlines but quietly shapes a shutout.

What it adds up to, on May 21, is a National League where Atlanta and Los Angeles continue to look like the class of their leagues while five teams in the Central remain bunched above .500 — a traffic jam that will eventually have to sort itself out, though not today. The American League is stranger: Tampa Bay leading by four and a half on a roster nobody quite trusts, Cleveland on a heater, and the West being run by a team with a negative run differential. The season is still young enough that these shapes will change. But the teams in trouble — the Angels, the Tigers, the Royals, the Astros, all losers of nine of their last ten or worse — are starting to look like teams in trouble, which is a different and harder thing to come back from than a slow start.

Don't bet your fantasy lineup on this. I've been wrong before.
