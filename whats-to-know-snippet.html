The American League West has quietly become the most compressed division in baseball, and Tuesday tightened the screws further. Seattle's Bryan Woo [6 IP, 2 ER, 9 K | 3.91 ERA, PAR 14] dismantled Houston at Daikin Park while Randy Arozarena [4-4, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 28] put on a personal exhibition, the Mariners pulling within a game of the first-place Athletics — who, it should be noted, just dropped a winnable one to St. Louis at home. Texas joined the party by handling Arizona behind eight innings of MacKenzie Gore [8 IP, 1 ER, 5 K | 4.50 ERA, PAR 9], leaving the top three teams in the division separated by a game and a half. The Astros, meanwhile, have now lost four straight and sit tied with the Angels at the division's bottom; for a team with their payroll and pedigree, that's not a slump, that's a problem.

Over in the National League West, the Giants did the Padres a favor by stomping the Dodgers at Dodger Stadium, with Eric Haase [2-4, 2 HR | RC 5] of all people supplying two home runs. Los Angeles has now lost four in a row, and the Padres' half-game lead in the division — despite their own loss in Milwaukee — feels sturdier than it did Monday morning. And in the AL East, the Yankees handled Baltimore behind a Trent Grisham [1-3, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 15] three-run shot, but they couldn't gain on Tampa Bay, which somehow keeps finding ways. More on that in a moment.

Because the Rays-Blue Jays game in Toronto was the day's masterpiece of chaos. Tampa Bay carried a four-run lead into the bottom of the seventh and watched it evaporate in a five-run Toronto rally that featured a Yohendrick Piñango [1-3 | RC 6] two-run double and a fielding error by Junior Caminero [2-6 | RC 26]. Then the Rays went and won it in extras anyway, scoring twice in the tenth — a 3.93 WPA swing and the kind of game that explains how a team outscoring its opponents by 24 runs has the best record in the league. The White Sox-Royals tilt at Rate Field was nearly as wild: Kansas City led, Chicago answered with a five-spot punctuated by Chase Meidroth [1-4, 1 HR | RC 20]'s three-run homer, Kansas City came back to tie, and then Derek Hill [1-1, 1 HR | RC 7] ended it with a walk-off shot in the eighth. Even the Phillies' win at Fenway had its own quiet drama, with Zack Wheeler [7.1 IP, 1 ER, 4 K | 2.60 ERA, PAR 9] and Boston's Brayan Bello [6.1 IP, 1 ER, 5 K | 6.46 ERA, PAR 0] trading zeroes until Jhoan Duran [1 IP, 0 ER, 2 K | 1.76 ERA, PAR 5] had to escape a bases-occupied ninth to nail it down.

A few performances worth pulling out of the box scores:

  • Minnesota's Bailey Ober [9 IP, 0 ER, 7 K | 3.46 ERA, PAR 14] threw a complete-game shutout against Miami, two hits, no walks, and the day's top PAR mark. Ninety-pitch efficiency is becoming an endangered species; appreciate it where you find it.
  • Pittsburgh's Paul Skenes [8 IP, 0 ER, 10 K | 1.98 ERA, PAR 21] did Paul Skenes things at PNC Park, going eight scoreless against Colorado with ten strikeouts and zero walks. He's now firmly in the NL Cy Young conversation, which surprises exactly nobody.
  • Seattle's Randy Arozarena went 4-for-4 with a homer in Houston. A perfect day at the plate from a leadoff-adjacent hitter in a 10-2 rout is the sort of thing that doesn't need embellishment.
  • Washington's Daylen Lile [3-5, 2 HR | RC 23] and Luis García Jr. [3-5, 2 HR | RC 15] each hit two homers in the Nationals' rout of Cincinnati. The Reds, by the way, have now lost eight of their last ten and are Pythagorean disasters — their run differential says they should be a 17-win team, not a 22-win team. Regression has a way of finding people.

The bigger picture: the NL Central has five teams above .500, which is either a testament to depth or an indictment of the bottom of the league, and probably both. Atlanta keeps humming along at a .690 clip and looks every bit the class of the National League. The AL Central, by contrast, has exactly one team above water in Cleveland, and the Guardians are 23-21. Someone has to win that division, I suppose. And spare a thought for the Reds and Cubs, both of whom managed to lose ground on Tuesday despite the Cubs sitting on a comfortable lead — Chicago has now dropped three straight, and Milwaukee, winners of five in a row, is breathing on their neck.
