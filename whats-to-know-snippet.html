The most consequential game of the day might have been the one that ended in a routine 6-3 box score line. The Cardinals' win over Milwaukee at Busch Stadium tightens what is now the most crowded division in baseball — the NL Central has all five teams above .500, and St. Louis pulled within two of the Cubs while pushing the Brewers down to fourth. Speaking of the Cubs, Michael Conforto [1-1, 1 HR | RC 7]'s ninth-inning homer at Wrigley sent Cincinnati to its fourth straight loss and gave Chicago a six-game winning streak. The Reds, who came in just three back, now sit three games out of first and watching the wrong direction.

Out west, San Francisco's win over San Diego at Oracle Park dropped the Padres a game and a half behind the Dodgers, who themselves rolled in Houston. The Mariners' walk-off-adjacent comeback against Atlanta, meanwhile, gained Seattle a half-game in the AL West on the Athletics, who lead a division in which exactly one team is above .500. That is not a sentence one writes with much enthusiasm.

The day's drama belonged to Wrigley Field. Down a run entering the ninth, the Cubs got a triple from Pete Crow-Armstrong [1-4 | RC 14] and then a walk-off solo shot from Conforto — the largest single WPA swing of the day at .464, followed by the third-largest at .357, both in the same half-inning. That's how you erase a deficit in style.

Seattle's sixth inning against Atlanta wasn't far behind. J.P. Crawford [1-3, 2 BB, 1 HR | RC 12] and Luke Raley [1-2, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 15] went back-to-back-ish for the Mariners, turning a three-run deficit into a two-run lead in a single frame. And in Detroit, the Red Sox sat scoreless through six before Jarren Duran [3-5, 1 HR | RC 11]'s three-run shot in the seventh broke the game open; Boston needed every bit of it, as the Tigers scratched back two in the eighth before Aroldis Chapman [1 IP, 0 ER, 1 K | 0.80 ERA, PAR 6] closed it.

A few performances worth singling out:

  • Davis Martin [7 IP, 0 ER, 10 K | 1.64 ERA, PAR 19] of the White Sox was the day's best pitcher, period — seven shutout innings with double-digit strikeouts and no walks at Angel Stadium. That's an ace's line.
  • Payton Tolle [7 IP, 0 ER, 8 K | 2.09 ERA, PAR 7] of Boston was nearly as good in Detroit, holding the Tigers to a single hit over seven innings. He earned the win when his offense finally woke up.
  • Aaron Nola [6 IP, 0 ER, 5 K | 5.09 ERA, PAR 6] threw six shutout innings in Miami in a 1-0 game. The Phillies' bullpen needed a Trea Turner [0-4 | RC 15]-to-Stott-to-Harper [3-4, 1 HR | RC 24] double play to seal it, but Nola made the lead hold up.
  • Munetaka Murakami [3-4, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 27] and Aaron Judge [2-4, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 33] are now tied atop the AL home run leaderboard at 14 apiece, both having gone deep yesterday. Andrew Benintendi [4-5 | RC 10]'s four-hit night in the same Chicago lineup deserves a nod too.

The Braves and Yankees remain the class of their respective leagues, both above .680, but the chase packs are sorting themselves out. The NL Central's depth is real — five teams over .500, with run differentials suggesting Milwaukee (+42) and Pittsburgh (+31) are better than their records show, while Cincinnati at -23 looks like a team running on fumes. In the AL East, the Red Sox and Orioles are now ten and nine games back respectively before May is a week old, which is not technically over but feels close to it.
