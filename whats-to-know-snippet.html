The most consequential game on the board was probably the one in St. Louis, where the Milwaukee Brewers jumped on the Cardinals for four in the first and never let go. The NL Central is the most crowded division in baseball — five teams above .500 — and Milwaukee's win, combined with the Cubs' walkoff over Cincinnati, tightens the chase behind Chicago while pushing the Reds further into a six-game tailspin. Andrew Vaughn [2-4, 1 HR | RC 2]'s first-inning three-run shot off St. Louis starter Andre Pallante [6 IP, 5 ER, 3 K | 4.37 ERA, PAR 6] was the whole ballgame in WPA terms, and Milwaukee's bullpen, led by Aaron Ashby [2 IP, 0 ER, 1 K | 2.35 ERA, PAR 9], did the rest.

Out west, the Padres' win at Oracle Park was a curious one for the standings: San Diego beat San Francisco and still managed to lose ground in the National League wild card race, which tells you something about how the rest of the NL is performing. The Dodgers' twelve-run thumping of Houston at Daikin Park keeps Los Angeles a half-game up on San Diego in the West and shoves the Astros further toward the cellar — Houston is now tied with the Angels at the bottom of an AL West where nobody has reached .500 yet. Speaking of which, both Texas's win at Yankee Stadium and Seattle's at home against Atlanta tightened that division to within a single game of the first-place Athletics. The AL West is a four-team scrum where the leader is 18-18.

The dramatic game of the night, by a wide margin, was the Cubs' walkoff over Cincinnati. The Reds came into the ninth trailing and scored four to take a 6-4 lead — only for Chicago's Pete Crow-Armstrong [2-3, 1 BB, 1 HR | RC 17] to homer in the bottom half and force extras, where Michael Busch [1-3, 2 BB | RC 17] drew a bases-loaded walk to end it. A 5.03 WPA swing is the kind of number you only see when a game changes hands two or three times in the late innings, and this one did. The Cubs have now won eight in a row.

The Phillies-Athletics game at Citizens Bank Park was the other genuine rollercoaster, with Philadelphia trailing into the eighth before a four-run rally — keyed by Edmundo Sosa [2-4 | RC 7]'s two-run single off Oakland reliever Jack Perkins [1.1 IP, 2 ER, 2 K | 3.80 ERA, PAR 5] — flipped a 3-2 deficit into a comfortable win. And down at loanDepot park, Baltimore and Miami traded blows from the first inning on, with Pete Alonso [1-5, 1 HR | RC 21]'s three-run shot in the top of the first essentially providing the margin of victory in a game that kept threatening to turn.

A few performances worth pulling out of the box scores:

  • Paul Skenes [8 IP, 0 ER, 7 K | 2.36 ERA, PAR 16] of the Pirates was the best pitcher on the field, and the field included Nathan Eovaldi [8 IP, 1 ER, 8 K | 4.19 ERA, PAR 8] and Bryan Woo [6 IP, 0 ER, 9 K | 4.02 ERA, PAR 11]. He threw eight shutout innings against Arizona at Chase Field with no walks, and Pittsburgh needed every bit of it to win 1-0.
  • Andy Pages [3-5, 3 HR | RC 29] of the Dodgers hit three home runs in Houston. There's not much more to say about that — three-homer games are rare enough that they deserve their own sentence.
  • Nathan Eovaldi went eight innings at Yankee Stadium, walked nobody, and held the Yankees to one run. Texas needed a starter to outpitch a tough lineup on the road, and he did.
  • CJ Abrams [3-5, 1 HR | RC 28] of the Nationals had five RBI in Washington's 15-2 demolition of Minnesota, one of four Nationals to homer in a game where seven different players scored multiple runs.

Zoom out and the picture is mostly the picture we already had: the Yankees and Rays are running away with the AL East (Tampa Bay's six-game winning streak has them within a half-game of New York), the Braves and Cubs lead their respective divisions comfortably, and the Dodgers and Padres are separated by a half-game out west. The standings oddity of the day remains Tampa Bay, whose Pythagorean record (19.8 expected wins) trails their actual record by more than four games — the Rays are getting outscored by their opponents on the season and sitting in second place anyway. That's not sustainable, but it's been going on long enough that you start to wonder. Meanwhile the Reds, who have lost six straight and just blew a ninth-inning lead at Wrigley, are still over .500 by virtue of how good their April was. The NL Central will sort itself out eventually, but not today.
